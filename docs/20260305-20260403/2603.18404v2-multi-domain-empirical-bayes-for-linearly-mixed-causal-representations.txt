## Abstract 

Causal representation learning (CRL) aims to learn low-dimensional causal latent variables from high-dimensional observations. While identifiability has been extensively studied for CRL, estimation has been less explored. In this paper, we explore the use of empirical Bayes (EB) to estimate causal rep-resentations. In particular, we consider the prob-lem of learning from data from multiple domains, where differences between domains are modeled by interventions in a shared underlying causal model. Multi-domain CRL naturally poses a si-multaneous inference problem that EB is designed to tackle. Here, we propose an EB f -modeling al-gorithm that improves the quality of learned causal variables by exploiting invariant structure within and across domains. Specifically, we consider a linear measurement model and interventional pri-ors arising from a shared acyclic SCM. When the graph and intervention targets are known, we de-velop an EM-style algorithm based on causally structured score matching. We further discuss EB g-modeling in the context of existing CRL ap-proaches. In experiments on synthetic data, our proposed method achieves more accurate estima-tion than other methods for CRL. 

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Causal representation learning (CRL) seeks to recover low-dimensional latent causal variables from high-dimensional realizations [ 1 , 29 , 39 ]. Since this task is highly challenging in general [ 19 , 26 ], a common approach is to leverage hetero-geneous (non-i.i.d.) data as a learning signal [ 20 , 50 ]. A cen-tral idea is that invariances under heterogeneity can reveal causal information [ 57 ]. A prominent example is grouped data from different environments or domains. From a causal perspective, such multi-domain data is typically viewed as arising from sparse interventions in an underlying causal model [ 32 , 33 ], while the remaining causal mechanisms and the measurement process are shared across domains. Most prior work on CRL, including in the multi-domain setting, has focused on the question of identifiabil-ity [ 2 , 7, 23 , 44 , 49 , 51 , 54 , 58 , 59 ]: under what conditions and up to what ambiguities can the latent causal variables provably be recovered at the population level, i.e., in the infinite-data regime? In this paper, we focus on the esti-mation problem . How can we learn causal representations from finite samples of noisy measurements? CRL naturally amounts to a simultaneous inference prob-lem: each observation has its own local latent variable, yet these variables follow a shared distribution induced by the underlying causal model. In the multi-domain CRL setting, such information sharing occurs within and across domains, since the measurement process and parts of the causal model are assumed to remain invariant. In statistics, the idea of empirical Bayes (EB) offers an el-egant solution to the problem of simultaneous inference of many local latent variables [ 15 , 21 , 38 , 55 ]. The canon-ical EB setup assumes all local latent variables are i.i.d. according to a shared and unknown prior, and then solves the problem of simultaneous inference via shrinkage [ 22 ]. Multi-domain CRL provides a novel version of this problem, where the local variables are the latent causal variables, and the domains exhibit structured heterogeneity through sparse interventions. We develop EB methods for this CRL setting. 

Overview and contributions. In this work, e adopt a prob-abilistic perspective on CRL with noisy measurements and focus on finite-sample estimation via EB. We first develop the connection between CRL and EB at the conceptual level of modeling principles (§ 2), and then study a concrete class of noisy multi-domain latent causal models (§ 3). Specifi-cally, we consider linear measurement models (§ 3.1) with interventional priors induced by an acyclic latent SCM with known causal graph and intervention targets (§ 3.2).

We then explore EB estimation for this model class. Under an orthogonality condition on the mixing matrix, projecting observations yields a normal means model, which facilitates an EM-style f -modeling EB approach (§ 4), wherein poste-rior denoising can be expressed through Tweedie’s formulas in terms of the score of the marginal distribution. We lever-age the causal graph structure to parameterize and efficiently estimate this score across domains (§ 5), yielding an iterative algorithm (Alg. 1) that alternates score estimation, Tweedie updates for denoising latents, and EM updates for the mixing matrix and noise variance. We then discuss the g -modeling EB approach and establish connections to existing methods that fall into this category (§ 6). Through empirical studies on synthetic interventional data (§ 7), we demonstrate that CRL f -modeling improves latent recovery relative to natural baselines and provides stable performance across domains.

We now describe the assumptions we place on the measure-ment model (§ 3.1) and domain-specific priors (§ 3.2).

We focus on the setting in which the measurement model is linear with additive, zero-mean, isotropic Gaussian noise.

The single-domain setting with 

dX > d Z and unknown A constitutes a generalization of this classical setting, which is related to probabilistic PCA [ 45 ]. 1

For multiple domains ( M > 1), Asm. 3.1 generalizes the classical setting, where each domain has a possibly differ-ent prior zei ∼ ge := p ⋆(z | ae). That is, an empirical Bayes setup plays out in each domain. However, there is not only information sharing within domains, through the shared priors ge, but also across domains, through the shared measurement model and shared causal structure among the domain-specific priors. This raises the following central questions, which we seek to answer in the present work. 

How can the assumed multi-domain structure be lever-aged to accurately estimate the shared measurement model and the latent variables? Can empirical Bayes estimation be generalized to the model from (3.1) ?

Rather than study these questions for arbitrary nonparamet-ric priors ge, we place additional structure on the domain-specific priors to incorporate the available information in the form of the observed action labels ae.

## 4 ESTIMATION AND INFERENCE 

Causal representation learning (CRL) is naturally a simul-taneous inference problem, as its goal is to recover the causal variables {zei }i∈[Ne], e ∈[M ] from the available multi-domain data D in Eq. (2.1). This amounts to PMe=1 Ne si-multaneous inference problems of computing the posteriors 

p⋆(zei | xei , ae) in Eq. (2.3), thus creating an opportunity to apply the empirical Bayes (EB) methodology. The main challenge for posterior inference is that the measurement model parameters A⋆ and σ2⋆ and the priors 

p⋆(z | ae) are unknown. 3 EB methods infer these quantities via Asm. 2.1 and fall into two categories, g-modeling and 

f -modeling, depending on how they handle the prior [21]. In g-modeling, the prior (typically denoted g) is modeled explicitly and estimated jointly with the other unknowns by approximately optimizing the marginal likelihood of D.The fitted model is then used for posterior inference. In f -modeling, the learning of the prior remains implicit. Instead, posterior quantities are estimated directly by mod-eling the marginal density (typically denoted f ) of the ob-served data [ 13 ]. This is often done via the classical Robbins-Tweedie formula, which uses the score of the marginal like-lihood to approximate the posterior mean and covariance of the local latent variables [11, 12, 28]. In the present work, we focus on f -modeling via Tweedie’s formula and adapt this method to our multi-domain CRL setting. As we will show, f -modeling provides a direct route to obtaining the posterior summaries of p⋆(z | x, a) by estimating the data distribution p⋆(x | a) in Eq. (2.2), without ever explicitly learning the domain-specific priors 

p⋆(z | ae). Specifically, we develop an expectation maxi-mization (EM) [ 9] algorithm for CRL f -modeling, which leverages a causally-structured score estimator. In the re-mainder of this section, we describe the main steps of this algorithm. Details on score estimation are deferred to § 5.

Further details of the simulation study are relayed to § D. 8 DISCUSSION 

We proposed an empirical Bayes f -modeling approach to multi-domain causal representation learning that addresses the simultaneous inference of latent causal variables under a noisy measurement model. The main EM algorithm performs iterative Tweedie updates with score matching. The empirical results show accurate performance. When the true DAG is small or when the DAG is unknown but a causal order is known, it may be safer to run CRL 

f -modeling with the complete DAG induced by that order, since it is well specified for the true score function. When the DAG is large, CRL f -modeling with the true DAG is typically the more scalable and accurate choice. For nonlinear measurement models, f -modeling is less di-rectly applicable because of the difficulty to adapt Tweedie’s formula. On the other hand, g-modeling becomes more natu-ral since it extends directly to nonlinear or learned decoders 

pθ (x | z), including deep networks. While our algorithm is rather focused on a particular sce-nario, we believe the framework of causal empirical Bayes to be more broadly applicable to other algorithms for other model classes. Extensions include richer intervention struc-tures p( z | a) with non-binary a (e.g., additive shifts) and relaxing the assumption of known graphs or targets by using the optimal EB objectives for graph/target discovery.

In this section, we review identifiability results of the latent variables z in CRL, with an emphasis on the linear measurement model with nonlinear latent causal model. Denote a generic estimator z given x by bz(x) : RdX 7 → RdZ .In representation learning, point identification is typically unattainable; instead, one often aims for set identification or identification up to an equivalence class [ 2, 29 , 57 ]. Identification results can be algorithm-agnostic and do not require a specific method to recover the parameters. The working model (3.1) corresponds to the setting of causal representation learning (CRL) with a linear measurement model, a general SCM, and known causal DAG and intervention targets. We now review some useful notions of identifiability to discuss our results. 

Definition E.1 (Element-identifiability / Disentanglement) . A learned representation bz ∈ RdZ is said to be element-identifiable if there exists a permutation matrix Pπ ∈ RdZ ×dZ and an element-wise diffeomorphism h(z) := (h1(z1), . . . , h dZ (zdZ )) ∈ RdZ such that 

bz = Pπ h(z⋆). (E.1) Element-identifiability is also known as perfect latent recovery in the language of Varici et al. [48] , where a representation bz

satisfying this condition is said to be perfect . It is also referred to as disentanglement in the sense of Moran and Aragam [29 ,Definition 5]. 

Definition E.2 (Scale-permutation-identifiability) . A learned representation bz ∈ RdZ is said to be scale-permutation-identifiable if there exists a permutation matrix Pπ ∈ RdZ ×dZ and a diagonal sign matrix D ∈ RdZ ×dZ such that 

bz = Pπ Dz ⋆. (E.2) Scale-permutation-identifiability is a stronger form of element-identifiability. It describes recovery of the ground-truth latent variables up to permutation and rescaling, which are common forms of ambiguities in linear latent variable models. 

Definition E.3 (Mixing-identifiablity) . A learned representation bz ∈ RdZ is said to be mixing-identifiable if there exists a permutation matrix Pπ ∈ RdZ ×dZ , a diagonal matrix D ∈ RdZ ×dZ and sparse matrix C ∈ RdZ ×dZ satisfying that Cjj = 0 

for all j ∈ [dZ ] and ch (i) ⊈ ch (j) = ⇒ Cij = 0 , ∀i ∈ [dZ ],

such that bz = Pπ (D + C)z⋆.This condition ensures that bz is constrained by the causal graph structure, where mixing occurs only among variables with nested parent sets. There has been extensive recent work on the question of identifiability in CRL. Under Asm. 3.1, identifiability typically holds only up to an equivalence class determined by the available interventions; for example, Squires et al. [44] establish such results under a linear measurement model together with a linear latent SCM. Several works clarify the limits and possibilities of identifiability. Under hard interventions and a linear measurement model, Squires et al. [44 , Prop. 2 ] and Buchholz et al. [7 , Remark 2 ] show that latent variables are identifiable only up to scaling and permutation, and that this is optimal without further assumptions on the causal variables z. For stochastic interventions, Varici et al. [49 , Thm. 14 ] show that under soft interventions and Asm. 3.1, one can identify latent variables up to mixing with their parents and recover the DAG up to transitive closure; moreover, if each node receives one hard intervention, then under a linear latent SCM both the DAG and the latent variables are identifiable (up to scaling) [ 49 , Thm. 16]. For general latent SCMs under soft interventions, Varici et al. [49 , Thm. 18 ] show that intervening on each node at least once suffices to recover the latent variables up to parent mixing. Beyond linear SCMs, Jin and Syrgkanis [23 , Thm. 3 ] establish recovery guarantees for general nonparametric SCMs via score-matching objectives, identifying latents up to componentwise monotone transformations under suitable conditions. In the fully nonparametric measurement setting, von Kügelgen et al. [51] provide identifiability guarantees (and impossibility results) showing that element-identifiability in the sense of Defn. E.1 is the sharp guarantee in general, even when one perfect intervention per node is available. We refer to Varici et al. [49] for a comprehensive study of identifiability results for multi-domain CRL across linear and nonparametric models and a range of interventional designs. We emphasize, however, that most of the aforementioned CRL identifiability theory does not assume the causal graph (or intervention targets) to be known, whereas our approach requires the true causal graph and intervention targets as inputs. This regime is studied in the setting of causal component analysis [54], who establish identifiability results for several classes of interventions in the known-graph setting.
