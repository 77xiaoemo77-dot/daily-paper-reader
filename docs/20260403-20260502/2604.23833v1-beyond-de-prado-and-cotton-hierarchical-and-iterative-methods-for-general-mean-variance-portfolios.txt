# Beyond De Prado and Cotton 

Hierarchical and Iterative Methods for General Mean–Variance Portfolios Bernd J. Wuebben AllianceBernstein, New York 

bernd.wuebben@alliancebernstein.com 

April 11, 2026 

Abstract 

Hierarchical Risk Parity [López de Prado, 2016] and the Schur-complement generalisation of Cotton [2024] are among the most widely adopted regularised portfolio construction methods, yet both are signal-blind: they solve only the minimum-variance problem and cannot accommodate an arbitrary expected-return forecast. This paper introduces three methods that incorporate alpha signals into hierarchical and regularised portfolio construction. 

HRP-μ is a hierarchical allocator that accepts an arbitrary signal μ and nests standard HRP when γ = 0 and μ = 1. It preserves the tree-based structure of HRP while extending it beyond the minimum-variance setting. HRP-Σμ strengthens this construction by replacing inverse-variance representatives with recursive local mean–variance optima, thereby using richer within-cluster covariance information at the same O(N 2) asymptotic cost. 

CRISP (Correlation-Regularised Iterative Shrinkage Portfolios) is an iterative solver for Pγ w = μ

with Pγ = (1 − γ) diag (Σ) + γΣ, so that γ interpolates between a diagonal portfolio rule and full Markowitz. At convergence, CRISP is Markowitz applied to a variance-preserving shrunk covariance—diagonal variances unchanged, off-diagonal correlations shrunk—with γ tuned for out-of-sample Sharpe rather than covariance-estimation loss. In Monte Carlo experiments across multiple covariance regimes and estimation ratios, HRP-μ

and HRP-Σμ both outperform plain HRP—not only on general expected-return forecasts, where HRP is signal-blind, but also on the minimum-variance problem μ = 1 that is HRP’s home territory—with HRP-Σμ consistently improving on HRP-μ. CRISP at intermediate γ is the dominant method in both regimes, outperforming HRP, Cotton, Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage, direct Markowitz, and the signal-aware hierarchical methods at every sample size tested. 

Keywords: portfolio optimization, hierarchical risk parity, Schur complement, signal-dependent allocation, covariance shrinkage, out-of-sample evaluation

1.1 Motivation 

Hierarchical Risk Parity [López de Prado, 2016] and the Schur-complement allocator of Cotton [2024] have become standard regularised portfolio constructions. Both are signal-blind . They solve only the minimum-variance problem μ = 1 and have no mechanism for an expected-return forecast. Cotton’s allocator connects HRP to exact minimum variance through a single continuous parameter 

γ ∈ [0 , 1] and is in that sense a strict generalisation of López de Prado, but it inherits the μ = 1

restriction and costs O(N 3/6) because a sibling covariance block must be inverted at every internal tree node. This restriction collides with three features of real portfolio construction. The first is signal integration . Quantitative equity, factor investing, tactical asset allocation, and Black–Litterman view blending [Black and Litterman, 1992; Grinold and Kahn, 1999; Barroso and Santa-Clara, 2015] all deliver a heterogeneous μ, and that signal is the object that distinguishes an active portfolio from a risk-only allocation. Setting μ = 1 is active discarding, not a neutral default. The second is 

computational tractability . Universes with N between 10 3 and 10 4, re-optimised on rolling windows, make O(N 3) operations costly; O(N 2) is the budget a realistic backtest can absorb. The third is 

estimation error . Direct Markowitz inversion [Markowitz, 1952] amplifies noise on low-variance eigenvectors and produces the extreme weights documented by Michaud [1989], and the standard remedy is structural shrinkage of the covariance estimator [Stein, 1956; Ledoit and Wolf, 2003, 2004, 2017, 2020; Chen et al., 2010]. This paper asks a single question. Can the hierarchical-and-shrinkage structure that makes HRP and the Cotton allocator useful be extended to the general mean–variance problem with arbitrary 

μ without giving up its regularisation benefits, and what is the natural iterative counterpart? The answer is three methods, each controlled by a shrinkage parameter γ ∈ [0 , 1] that plays the same role Cotton [2024] introduced—governing how much cross-asset covariance enters the allocation—and each accepting an arbitrary signal.

1.3 Three supporting contributions 

Adaptive γ⋆ rule. A bias–variance decomposition of expected OOS Sharpe yields the closed-form rule 

γ⋆ ≈ 11 + c κ (C)2 N/ (T IC 2) ,

whose comparative statics (Proposition 7.1) are qualitatively correct: γ⋆ rises with the sample length T and the information coefficient IC , and falls with conditioning difficulty κ(C) and universe size N . Calibration on a 48 -cell synthetic cube shows that the OOS Sharpe surface is nearly flat in 

γ: the plateau of near-optimal values has median width 0.38 on [0 , 1] . The formula’s practical value 6is therefore explanatory—it rationalises why a fixed default γ ≈ 0.5 is robust and why a single knob suffices—rather than prescriptive. 

Three regularisation channels. CRISP regularises through three distinct channels (Remark 7.2). Channel 1 is operator shrinkage via γ: the statistical channel that does most of the work. Channel 2 is convergence slack from a finite sweep count, which is a computational artefact rather than a design choice. Channel 3 is implicit spectral truncation through early stopping, which is statistically beneficial near γ = 1 because the low-eigenvalue directions of D−1Pγ converge last and carry the most estimation noise; in that regime CRISP is an iterative cousin of ridge regression on the Markowitz system. At the recommended operating point (γ ≈ 0.5, p = 100) only Channel 1 is materially active. 

Out-of-sample evaluation. Every method comparison is out-of-sample. We run a synthetic Monte Carlo study across four covariance regimes (factor, block, spiked, equicorrelation) and 

T /N ∈ [0 .6, 5] , with both oracle and sample-mean signal treatments, against 1/N [DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal, 2009], HRP, Cotton at several γ, direct Markowitz, and linear and non-linear Ledoit–Wolf [Ledoit and Wolf, 2004, 2020], in addition to our three methods. Direction error appears only as an in-sample tracking diagnostic; OOS Sharpe is the primary metric. 

1.4 Memory, not speed 

It is worth stating early what CRISP does and does not offer at the machine level. On tuned BLAS—Apple M4 with Accelerate and a Numba-JIT CRISP—direct Cholesky is 48 × faster than CRISP at N = 500 and remains between 9× and 48 × faster for N ∈ { 500 , . . . , 5,000 } (Remark 5.7). Extrapolating the trend on this hardware places the single-solve wall-clock crossover near N ≈ 45 ,000 ,well outside realistic portfolio sizes. We do not claim a wall-clock advantage for CRISP. What CRISP offers structurally is memory. A factor-streaming implementation (Algorithm 3) that never materialises Σ reduces working memory from O(N 2) to O(N K ), where K is the number of factors. At N = 30 ,000 and K = 20 the dense Σ occupies 7.2 GB; the factor stream uses 

4.8 MB. Cholesky cannot exploit this reduction because its factorisation needs random access to the full matrix. The honest case for CRISP is therefore stronger OOS Sharpe together with memory scalability, not speed. 

1.5 Road map 

Section 2 fixes notation, sets up the ray view of mean–variance, and introduces the direction-error diagnostic together with its sign-invariance caveat. Section 3 reviews HRP and the Cotton Schur allocator and places both in the method landscape, including Cotton’s intermediate-γ instability under estimation noise. Section 4 develops the two tree-based methods in a single frame—one tree, two representatives, two methods: HRP-μ with the signed inverse-variance representative, HRP-Σμ

with the recursive local MVO. Section 5 is the headline theoretical section: it presents CRISP, proves unconditional Gauss–Seidel convergence and the correlation-conditioning rate theorem, records the early-stopping-as-spectral-truncation remark, describes the factor-streaming variant, and locates CRISP within the Ledoit–Wolf family. Section 6 develops the perturbation theory of the shrinkage trajectory, including a non-monotone-trajectory counter-example and an interior-γ⋆ proposition under explicit hypotheses. Section 7 derives the adaptive γ⋆ rule and consolidates the three-channel taxonomy. Section 8 presents the five-method comparative landscape and a practitioner’s guide. Section 9 treats linear constraints through a projected CRISP. Section 10 reports the synthetic 7Monte Carlo study in three parts: in-sample direction-error diagnostics, out-of-sample Sharpe panels, and sweep-count and adaptive-γ⋆ calibration. Section 11 discusses limitations, open problems, and extensions, and Section 12 concludes. Readers primarily interested in using the methods can skip to Sections 5, 4, and 10. 

1.6 Relation to the literature 

The paper connects six strands of research. 

Hierarchical portfolio methods. López de Prado [2016] introduces HRP, and López de Prado and Lewis [2019] develops companion tools for false-strategy detection. Raffinot [2018] studies broader hierarchical-clustering allocation. Cotton [2024] provides the Schur-complement unification that connects HRP to exact minimum variance through a single continuous parameter. Our HRP-μ

and HRP-Σμ extend this hierarchical framework to arbitrary μ at the same O(N 2) cost, and strictly dominate HRP on its own μ = 1 problem whenever estimation error is present. 

Covariance shrinkage. The Stein estimator [Stein, 1956] and its portfolio-theoretic descendants [Ledoit and Wolf, 2003, 2004, 2017, 2020; Chen et al., 2010] shrink the sample covariance toward a structured target to control low-eigenvalue estimation error. CRISP is a member of this family with target D = diag (Σ), a choice that preserves variances identically and shrinks only correlations, and with shrinkage intensity tuned for portfolio Sharpe rather than Frobenius loss on the estimator. Both differences are structural; we develop them in Section 5. 

Iterative and parametric portfolio methods. Brandt, Santa-Clara and Valkanov [2009] parameterise the policy directly as a linear function of asset characteristics. Kozak, Nagel and Santosh [2020] shrink the cross-section of factor returns through ridge and Bayesian regularisation. DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal [2009] establish the 1/N benchmark that any proposed method must outperform out of sample. CRISP differs from these methods by solving a shrunk Markowitz system whose variances are preserved identically at every γ, so no asset is implicitly up- or down-volatilised by the regulariser. 

Signal integration and Bayesian portfolios. Black and Litterman [1992] combine an equilib-rium prior with investor views through a Bayesian posterior; Grinold and Kahn [1999] formalise the information-ratio decomposition of active management; and Barroso and Santa-Clara [2015] study signal-conditioned portfolios in currency markets. We compare CRISP with Black–Litterman as a candidate Bayesian reading in Section 5: both produce shrunk posterior weights, but CRISP is driven by the single explicit γ knob rather than by a specified view covariance. 

Numerical analysis of iterative methods. The classical iterative-solver literature [Hackbusch, 2016; Saad, 2003; Varga, 2000; Young, 1971; Ostrowski, 1954] supplies the unconditional-convergence and rate results for symmetric positive definite Gauss–Seidel on which Theorems 5.2 and 5.3 rest. The contribution of Theorem 5.3 is to extract the correlation-conditioning constant specific to the portfolio setting and to translate the resulting rate into a concrete sweep count that is independent of the universe size. 8Factor models for covariance. Fama and French [1993] establish the pricing-factor representa-tion. Fan, Liao and Mincheva [2013] develop POET for structured covariance estimation, Marchenko and Pastur [1967] provide the spectral baseline for sample covariances, and Ledoit and Wolf [2020] give the analytical non-linear-shrinkage counterpart. Our factor-streaming variant of CRISP exploits a factor structure Σ = BΛB⊤ + Didio to hold working memory at O(N K ) without ever forming Σ,which is what makes the method usable in universes where Cholesky’s random-access requirement is the binding constraint. We do not replace any of these literatures. We supply an O(κ(C) N 2 log ε−1) iterative solver that inherits HRP’s regularisation philosophy through its γ parameter, two signal-aware hierarchical alternatives for tree-interpretable use cases, and a synthetic out-of-sample evaluation against the standard benchmarks. 

# 2 Notation and the direction view 

This section does four things. It fixes the symbols that appear in the rest of the paper; it replaces the usual relative-error metric with a scale-invariant direction error and flags the one way in which that replacement is dangerous; it isolates the correlation conditioning κ(C) as the spectral invariant that will control both the in-sample direction gap of the cheapest baseline and the sweep count of CRISP; and it records a parallel-direction identity that exposes a family of signal vectors on which every method in the paper returns the same ray. The last point is a pruning lemma: it cuts off an entire class of otherwise plausible worst-case constructions.

2.4 Warning: direction error is sign-invariant 

The same property that makes dir robust to normalisation makes it blind to sign. The squared inner product in (5) gives

For methods whose sign relative to w⋆ is pinned by construction—HRP, Cotton, HRP-μ, HRP-Σμ, and CRISP all fall in this category—this is harmless. For methods without that guarantee it is not. The canonical example is the sum-normalised recursive MVO (the Method A1 of Appendix C): on the noiseless recovery problem it appears, on dir , to be tracking w⋆ well, while in fact half the time it is delivering systematically negative out-of-sample Sharpe. For this reason we treat dir as a tracking diagnostic , not a quality metric. It is the right tool for asking “does my method stay near the Markowitz ray as γ moves or as T grows?” It is the wrong tool for ranking methods. The primary quality metric for ranking is out-of-sample Sharpe, and Section 10 is organised around it for this reason.

HRP and Cotton together give a clean story for μ = 1. But that signal is, in applied portfolio construction, the degenerate corner of the mean–variance problem. Four representative settings make the point. 

Quant equity and factor investing. Cross-sectional factor signals—momentum, value, quality, earnings revisions, low-volatility—produce heterogeneous expected returns across names, and the entire point of a factor model is that μ̸ = 1. An allocator that ignores μ throws away the primary input before the optimisation begins. 

Multi-signal integration. Modern quant pipelines aggregate many orthogonalised signals into a composite alpha [Grinold and Kahn, 1999]. The composite is a rich μ; the portfolio stage is asked to weigh it against covariance risk. A signal-blind allocator is simply inadmissible in this context. 

Tactical asset allocation. Multi-asset allocators take views on expected returns at the asset-class level—equities versus bonds versus commodities versus alternatives—and expect the optimiser to translate those views into positions. μ is low-dimensional here but highly informative; setting it to 

1 is not a simplification, it is a replacement of the problem. 

Black–Litterman view integration. The Black–Litterman framework [Black and Litterman, 1992] returns a posterior μ that encodes a prior together with investor views. The value of the framework is entirely in that posterior; an allocator that discards it is not solving the same problem. In each case μ = 1 is not a neutral default. It is an active discarding of the most informative input, and any method restricted to it is, by construction, inapplicable to the bulk of active portfolio construction.

HRP-μ (Section 4.3) is the transparent one: it uses a signed inverse-variance portfolio as the representative, which preserves the block-diagonal recovery of De Prado HRP at μ = 1 and is provably stable for arbitrary signal vectors. HRP-Σμ (Section 4.4) is the strongest tree method we know: it uses a recursive mean–variance optimum as the representative and rescues the natural but sign-pathological recursion of Appendix C by replacing sum-to-one with 

L1 normalisation. Both methods cost O(N 2), both nest HRP at their γ = 0 , μ = 1 limits, and both carry an explicit signal through the tree in a direction-consistent way. The section closes with two short remarks that tie the construction to the rest of the paper: why iterating the tree pass diverges (which motivates CRISP in Section 5), and how the tree-based methods relate to the negative result on sum-normalised recursive MVO that is documented in full in Appendix C.

4.5 Iterating the tree pass diverges 

It is tempting to iterate: after one bottom-up pass produces weights w(n), update the representative at each node to reflect w(n), re-run the pass, and loop. For an exact block solver this outer iteration would converge. For HRP-μ and HRP-Σμ it does not. The reason is structural: each tree pass compresses the |L| × | R| cross-block covariance ΣLR into a single scalar c at every node, and this compression is an approximation of the exact block inverse, not an exact identity. The outer iteration amplifies the approximation error rather than correcting it; empirically it diverges on the same synthetic panels on which a single pass is stable. Recovering convergence would require replacing the scalar c with the full block inverse, restoring an O(N 3) cost per pass with multiple passes on top—which erases the quadratic-cost advantage that motivated the tree in the first place. The tension between approximation fidelity and iteration convergence inside the tree is exactly what CRISP resolves by abandoning the dendrogram altogether and iterating in asset space instead; see Section 5, Theorem 5.2.

We use the quasi-diagonalised ordering in experiments but the theoretical rate does not rely on it.

The three-channel picture resolves an otherwise puzzling asymmetry. From results/sec09_sweep/ convergence_table.csv , the OOS Sharpe penalty at γ = 1 is 17 × larger under an oracle μ than under a noisy ˆμ: the oracle-μ peak-to-converged gap is 0.253 , while the noisy-ˆμ gap is 0.015 . Naively, one might expect noise in ˆμ to worsen the penalty. The information-theoretic reading gives the explanation. Under oracle μ, all information flows through ˆΣ, so any corruption from ˆΣ−1 amplification at γ = 1 is maximally costly. Under noisy 

ˆμ, the signal noise already diffuses portfolio direction across eigenvectors—it pre-regularises the portfolio against ˆΣ noise by blurring the signal’s spectral profile. Early stopping (Channel 3) is therefore most beneficial exactly when μ has real information worth protecting. This is prediction P6 in the pre-registered block (Section 10.4.1); the three-channel decomposition of Remark 7.2 predicts both the sign and order of magnitude of the asymmetry.

8.4 Key relationships 

Cotton vs. HRP-μ/HRP-Σμ. Cotton and the HRP-μ family share endpoints when μ = 1 — a tree allocation at γ = 0 and an approximation to Σ−11 at γ = 1 — but trace different γ-continua between them. A user comfortable with block inversion on each branch may still prefer HRP-Σμ for pure speed on large trees. 

Cotton vs. CRISP. Cotton and CRISP both reach Σ−11 at γ = 1 when μ = 1, via completely different routes: recursive block inversion on the tree at O(N 3) for Cotton, flat Gauss–Seidel iteration at O(κN 2 log ε−1) for CRISP. For a general μ̸ = 1 the comparison ceases to be meaningful because Cotton is undefined; there is no heterogeneous-signal extension of Cotton’s Schur pass.

HRP itself sits at the intersection of all three “do-nothing” choices; the methodological contributions of this paper are the systematic relaxation of each axis in turn.

8.6 When not to use a tree 

Two regimes render a tree-based method unattractive. First, when κ(C) is small, every method in Table 7 produces similar portfolios because the off-diagonal structure a hierarchical splitter exploits is weak; CRISP at moderate γ remains the cleanest default since it still benefits from shrinkage and does not require building a dendrogram. Second, when N is very large and Σ is dense, Cotton’s 

O(N 3) cost is prohibitive; HRP-μ and HRP-Σμ remain O(N 2) but are dominated on Sharpe by CRISP, which is also O(N 2) per sweep and, critically, can exploit the factor-streaming variant of Algorithm 3 to collapse working memory from O(N 2) to O(N K ). In either regime, CRISP wins not because hierarchical methods fail but because, in the synthetic experiments of Section 10, it combines stronger out-of-sample performance with the absence of a dendrogram constraint.

9.2 Projected CRISP 

CRISP extends cleanly to (52) through projected Gauss–Seidel . Let C denote the closed convex feasible set defined by the budget, box, and linear-inequality blocks of (52) , and let ΠC be orthogonal projection onto C. After each coordinate update we clamp to the box; at the end of each sweep we project onto C. Algorithm 4 states the procedure. Convergence. Under the SPD assumption on Pγ that underwrites Theorem 5.2, the unconstrained Gauss–Seidel map is a contraction in the Pγ -induced norm, and ΠC is nonexpansive in the Euclidean norm. Composition with a similarity transform gives a contraction onto the constrained minimiser; the O(N 2) per-sweep cost is preserved, with the projection step a lower-order correction in all cases of practical interest. The cost of the projection step depends on C:

• Pure box + budget. ΠC reduces to a 1D simplex-style renormalisation and re-clamp, implementable in O(N log N ).

• Sector groups. ΠC becomes a small QP in the group dimension (one per group), negligible 48 against the O(N 2) sweep. 

• Active-set variant. Classify constraints by KKT residuals at the start of each sweep, freeze coordinates on the active face, and run the unconstrained sweep on the complement. This mirrors standard active-set QP practice while retaining O(N 2) per sweep outside the active set.

9.3 Constrained HRP-μ and HRP-Σμ

The tree methods accommodate two constraint families cleanly. 

Long-only. At each internal node, project the between-branch split α onto [0 , 1] ; within-branch weights are clamped to [0 , ∞) at the leaf solve. For HRP-Σμ the node-level 2 × 2 QP is replaced by its box-constrained variant: if both unconstrained components are nonnegative, accept the interior optimum; otherwise clamp the negative component to zero and allocate fully to the positive side. Both variants preserve the recursive O(N log N ) structure. 

Sector caps, tree-aligned. When the hierarchy is sector-aligned, a cap on a sector’s total weight is a cap on the cumulative budget flowing to the corresponding subtree, enforced by a one-dimensional clip on the α-products along the root-to-subtree path. Tree-aligned constraints are essentially free. Constraints that cut across the tree—arbitrary sector caps not aligned with the hierarchy, factor-exposure inequalities, turnover against a non-aligned incumbent—do not admit clean recursion. For such problems, projected CRISP of Section 9.2 is strictly preferable.

Four caveats are worth making explicit. First, constraint handling is not a central contribution of this paper. The role of this section is to show that the HRP-μ/HRP-Σμ/CRISP machinery degrades gracefully; we do not claim projected CRISP outperforms a modern QP solver on heavily constrained problems. For problems dominated by constraint activity—index replication with dozens of active inequalities—a specialised active-set or interior-point solver remains the right tool. The statistical contribution transports cleanly through (53). Second, the tree methods handle only tree-aligned constraints cleanly. Long-only, box bounds, and sector caps on aligned subtrees fit the recursion; general linear inequalities that cut across the hierarchy do not. For heavily constrained problems the tree methods are therefore not the right tool; the CRISP shrinkage idea—either via (53) or via projected CRISP—is. Third, the direction-error analysis of Section 6 does not apply directly to constrained problems. That analysis rests on the ray invariance of the Pγ family: scaling a solution by a positive constant preserves direction. A constraint set like ℓ ≤ w ≤ u is not scale-invariant, so a uniformly scaled direction may lie inside the box at one scale and outside at another. Adapting the direction metric to the active face of C is an interesting question but non-trivial, and we flag it as future work in Section 11. Fourth, the out-of-sample evaluation in Section 10 is primarily unconstrained; long-short results isolate the shrinkage-trajectory effect and let us compare direction errors cleanly. Long-only comparisons using Algorithm 4 are noted but relegated to Appendix E, or to future work.

10.5 Summary of Section 10 

With p = 100 sweeps and γ ∈ [0 .5, 0.7] , CRISP delivers 80 –94% of oracle Sharpe across two signal families (generic random μ and structural sector tilts), four sample sizes ( T ∈ { 60 , 120 , 240 , 500 }), and the minimum-variance limit μ = 1. It is numerically stable on every regime tested, including 59 the hedged tight-block covariance ( κ(C) ≈ 5.75 × 10 4) where 1/N and HRP return essentially noise and where Cotton exhibits its first documented small-T failure mode. HRP-Σμ is the dominant tree-based method: 20 –35% over HRP-μ on general μ, a tie with HRP-μ at the signal-blind point, and ≈ 90% of CRISP at pure O(N 2) cost with no iteration. HRP and Cotton are signal-blind and deliver OOS Sharpe near zero on any μ̸ = 1. The adaptive rule of Section 7 is explanatorily correct in its directional predictions but quantitatively weak; the OOS Sharpe surface is flat enough that 

γ ≈ 0.5 is a near-universal default. These conclusions set up the discussion in Section 11. 

# 11 Discussion 

This section is reflective, not generative. We revisit the three regularisation channels that organise the paper, list open problems and natural extensions, enumerate the limitations of the present study, and close with a methodological comment on the use of direction error as a diagnostic. No new results are introduced. 

11.1 Three regularisation channels revisited 

Remark 7.2 and Section 7.6 identify three distinct mechanisms by which CRISP damps estimation-error amplification: operator shrinkage through γ (Channel 1), statistical slack from finite sweep count p (Channel 2), and eigendirection-ordered convergence of Gauss–Seidel acting as an implicit spectral truncation (Channel 3, Remark 5.6). The (γ, p ) sweep of Table 20 dissects the three channels on the same synthetic design used elsewhere in the paper, and the pre-registered predictions P1, P2, P3, and P6 each isolate one channel: P1 probes Channel 1 at low γ, P2 certifies that Channel 2 is dormant at the recommended operating point, P3 exposes Channel 3 at γ = 1 through an interior 

p⋆, and P6 reveals how the clean-signal regime tilts the balance between Channels 1 and 3. The practical takeaway is compact: at (γ = 0 .5, p = 100) CRISP uses Channel 1 only; at γ = 1 it relies on Channel 3; in between, mixtures. For the practitioner this collapses to a single tunable knob, γ,with p = 100 a universal default; Channels 2 and 3 are computational conveniences rather than statistical parameters in the interior regime. The tree methods HRP-μ and HRP-Σμ expose only Channel 1 and so inherit the simpler tuning story by construction. 

11.2 Signed HSP as a standalone method 

Signed Hierarchical Signal Parity (HSP), defined in Section 4.3.1 as the γ = 0 endpoint of HRP-

μ, is mathematically a trivial consequence of the constructions in Section 4, but it occupies an interesting practitioner niche. It is a drop-in upgrade for users of López de Prado [2016] HRP who now hold an expected-return signal and wish to use it without sacrificing the tree-based interpretability, dependency-free implementation, and ill-conditioning robustness of the original. All of HRP’s auditable recursive structure is preserved; the only change is a signed inverse-variance representative at each node. We do not run a dedicated head-to-head out-of-sample study of signed HSP against HRP on a realistic cross-section of live alpha signals. Such a study — ideally including Black–Litterman and Ledoit–Wolf Markowitz as further baselines — is a natural follow-up. The mathematical content is settled by Section 4; the open question is empirical. 

11.3 Open problems 

We record seven open problems raised by the analysis. 60 1. Sharp worst-case μ (closed form). For fixed correlation C with condition number κ(C),we do not have a closed form for the signal μ maximising the direction error of P −1 

> γ

μ at a given 

γ. The classical Kantorovich ratio (κ − 1) /(κ + 1) is loose because it does not exploit the γ-dependence of Pγ . A sharp closed form would tighten Theorem 6.1 and replace sampling-based worst-case envelopes with analytic ones. 2. Sharp interior optimum γ⋆(p). Proposition 6.6 establishes that γ⋆ ∈ (0 , 1) under explicit non-degeneracy hypotheses, but the proof is existential. Numerical sweeps across the block, factor, and Toeplitz families are consistent with the scaling 

γ⋆(p) = 1 − Θ((p κ (C)) −1/2),

with the implied constant depending on the eigenvalue profile of C. We conjecture this rate but do not prove it; a proof would elevate the adaptive-γ⋆ rule from calibrated heuristic to parameter-free prescription. 3. Tighter Gauss–Seidel rate for factor-structured covariances. Theorem 5.3 gives the standard 1 − c/κ contraction for SPD Gauss–Seidel, sharp for generic SPD matrices but loose for block and banded operators. For factor-structured C = ΛΛ ⊤ + D, Young’s SOR theory [Young, 1971] suggests a √κ improvement is available. Establishing the corresponding rate for Pγ and its constrained projections would justify SOR over plain GS in CRISP’s inner loop and would sharpen Corollary 5.5. 4. Bayesian interpretation of CRISP. Is there a prior over (μ, Σ) under which the posterior mean of the Markowitz ray equals P −1 

> γ

μ? If so, γ acquires a principled interpretation as prior strength and CRISP joins the methodological lineage of Black and Litterman [1992] with a shrinkage-strength parameter in place of Black–Litterman’s view-confidence τ . We conjecture an inverse-Wishart-like family centred on the diagonal of Σ is the natural candidate, but leave the construction to future work. 5. Ledoit–Wolf correspondence. Synthetic experiments show close numerical agreement between the Sharpe-optimal γ⋆ and the Frobenius-optimal Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage intensity in the range γ⋆ ∈ [0 .5, 0.7] , matching to within a few percent. A clean analytic equivalence between the two objects — if it exists — would unify iterative shrinkage with the covariance-shrinkage literature of Ledoit and Wolf [2003, 2017]. 6. Approximation gap of HRP-Σμ. HRP-Σμ is exact on diagonal Σ (Proposition 4.10) but only approximately recovers HRP under the γ = 0 , μ = 1 conditions that define it ( cos ≈ 0.992 ;exact for trees of depth ≤ 2). The dependence of the approximation gap on tree structure and 

κ(C) is characterised empirically in Section 8 but not theoretically. 7. Sum-normalisation sign-flip as a theorem. Appendix C exhibits noiseless problems on which the sum-normalised recursive MVO returns a sign-flipped portfolio. A theorem characterising the covariance–signal pairs that produce flips would sharpen the negative-result contribution and make precise why sum normalisation is structurally, rather than merely numerically, fragile. 

11.4 Extensions 

A non-exhaustive list of natural extensions. 61 • Rank-constrained signals. When μ is known to lie in a low-dimensional factor subspace, CRISP’s Gauss–Seidel sweeps can be restricted to that subspace, reducing per-sweep cost from O(N 2) to O(N r ) with r the signal rank. 

• Online and streaming ̂ Σ. In rolling backtests and live production, warm-starting CRISP from the previous rebalance’s solution and running a handful of sweeps per update turns the iterative solver into an online method whose amortised cost is a small multiple of a matrix–vector product. 

• GPU implementation. Each sweep reduces to a matrix–vector product against Pγ , which parallelises trivially on GPU. For universes of several thousand names this is likely the largest single engineering win available to the framework. 

• Multi-period mean–variance with transaction costs. The present framework is single-period. Integration with the multi-period dynamic-trading setup of Gârleanu and Pedersen [2013] is an open direction; the shrinkage trajectory would need to be reinterpreted as a state-dependent object rather than a one-shot choice. 

11.5 Computational considerations 

The computational case for CRISP is primarily about memory and structure exploitation, not wall-clock at moderate N . Tuned-BLAS Cholesky on a modern machine solves the dense Markowitz system in under a millisecond at N = 500 , and nothing in this paper beats it on that axis. The shrinkage iterate does, however, require only O(N 2) working memory against Cholesky’s ∼ 2N 2, and more importantly it accommodates the factor-streaming variant (Section 5.6, Algorithm 3), which reduces working memory to O(N K ) when Σ admits a K-factor decomposition. At the universe scale — N in the tens of thousands, K in the dozens — this is a three-orders-of-magnitude reduction and the difference between a workstation and a dedicated server. Cholesky cannot exploit factor structure in the same way because the factorisation requires random access to the dense Σ and returns a dense triangular factor. The computational argument for CRISP lives here, not in the FLOP crossover. 

11.6 Limitations 

We list the main limitations of the present study. 1. Gaussian synthetic returns. The synthetic experiments of Section 10 use Gaussian returns. Real equity returns are heavier-tailed, and direction-error and OOS-Sharpe rankings could move under an elliptical or Student-t data-generating process. We do not carry out explicit heavy-tailed simulation alongside the Gaussian case. 2. No Bayesian treatment of μ. The signal is taken as given, either as an oracle or as a sample mean. We do not place a principled prior on μ itself and do not distinguish signal uncertainty from covariance uncertainty in any theorem. A Black–Litterman-style treatment [Black and Litterman, 1992] would interact non-trivially with the conjectured Bayesian interpretation of CRISP flagged in Section 11.3. 3. Tree sensitivity not foregrounded in the main text. Clustering choices (single / average / Ward linkage, correlation-based versus Euclidean distance, number of levels) affect every tree-based method in the paper (HRP, HRP-μ, HRP-Σμ, signed HSP). Robustness across 62 these choices is reported in Appendix E but the main-text analysis beyond Section 8 uses a single default clustering. 4. Approximate HRP recovery for HRP-Σμ. At γ = 0 and μ = 1, HRP-Σμ recovers HRP only approximately ( cos ≈ 0.992 ; exact for trees of depth ≤ 2). HRP-μ recovers HRP exactly under the same conditions (Proposition 4.2). The gap is small enough to be practically irrelevant but large enough to preclude strict drop-in replacement in settings where exact HRP replication is contractually required. 5. Cotton comparison restricted to its native problem. Our comparison with Cotton [2024] is run on the minimum-variance problem for which the Cotton allocator is defined. We do not test Cotton-style modifications for general mean–variance settings; such extensions would warrant a separate study. 6. No real-data backtest. This paper is synthetic Monte Carlo and in-sample diagnostics only. A CRSP- or Russell-style out-of-sample backtest is deliberately out of scope for the present version and is flagged as future work. The synthetic design was chosen to expose the direction-error and OOS-Sharpe behaviour cleanly; layering real-data frictions (delisting, transaction costs, heavy tails, regime shifts) on top is a substantial separate exercise. 

11.7 Broader methodological reflection: direction error as a diagnostic 

A final comment on methodology. The technical spine of the paper is the direction metric on Markowitz rays (Definition 2.1), and the core structural observation is that the shrinkage family Pγ

preserves the direction of μ when the correlation is trivial and otherwise rotates it continuously with vanishing residual at γ = 1 (Theorem 6.1, Corollary 6.3). The ray view is mathematically natural: mean–variance portfolios are defined up to positive scaling by a budget constraint, so scale-invariant distance between two portfolios is the correct object of study, and the metric is compact. Scale invariance is, however, a two-edged virtue. A metric that identifies w with any positive multiple of w is one step away from a metric that identifies w with −w, and that second identification would be blind to the most dangerous failure mode of any portfolio construction: returning the right magnitude on the wrong side of the market. Appendix C exhibits noiseless problems on which the sum-normalised recursive MVO does exactly this, and a naively sign-invariant diagnostic would score those failures as near-perfect. It was easy to miss — we missed it ourselves in early drafts, and the bug persisted precisely because the summary metric looked healthy. The methodological lesson is simple: scale-invariant metrics must be paired with sign-sensitive companions before being trusted as quality measures. In this paper we pair the unsigned direction error with three sign-sensitive diagnostics — signed cosine, coordinate-wise sign-match fraction, and signed direction angle on (−π, π ] — and we present them alongside out-of-sample Sharpe so that tracking (an in-sample quantity) is never separated from quality (an out-of-sample quantity). A method that tracks an oracle perfectly in unsigned direction but loses Sharpe is telling a different story from one that gains Sharpe while drifting in direction, and the reader should see both axes at once. We encourage future work on portfolio construction to adopt the same discipline: for every scale-invariant summary metric, report a sign-sensitive companion and an out-of-sample quality metric, before drawing any conclusion about which method is better. 63 12 Conclusion 

The gap and how we fill it 

HRP and Cotton are signal-blind: both solve the minimum-variance problem μ = 1 and discard the expected-return signal that every active process produces. We close that gap with three signal-aware allocators—HRP-μ, HRP-Σμ, and CRISP—unified by a single shrinkage parameter γ ∈ [0 , 1] that controls how much cross-asset correlation enters the allocation. At (γ, μ) = (0 , 1) the three recover the signal-blind benchmarks exactly. 

Three methods, one spectrum 

The methods form a spectrum of increasing covariance exploitation at increasing cost and decreasing transparency. HRP-μ (Section 4.3) uses a signed inverse-variance representative; it is closed-form, 

O(N 2), preserves the hierarchical audit trail, and recovers 58 –67% of oracle Sharpe. HRP-Σμ

(Section 4.4) replaces the signed IVP with a local mean–variance optimum, giving the strongest tree-based method at roughly 90% of CRISP’s Sharpe. CRISP (Section 5) abandons the tree and solves the shrunk system Pγ w = μ by Gauss–Seidel, dominating every benchmark at 80 –94% of oracle Sharpe. 

The core statistical insight 

Solve the shrunk Markowitz system, not the exact one. The shrinkage at γ < 1 damps the estimation noise that full inversion amplifies, and the effect is statistical, not computational: it holds whether the solver is faster or slower than Cholesky. The OOS Sharpe surface is nearly flat in γ across a wide plateau, so γ = 0 .5 is a robust default. At the recommended operating point the sweep count 

p is a computational parameter, not a statistical one—OOS Sharpe is flat from p ≈ 50 onward. 

The practitioner’s diagnostic: κ(C)

The single most informative number to compute before choosing a method is κ(C) = λmax (C)/λ min (C),the condition number of the correlation matrix. It governs both the directional difficulty of the portfolio problem and the iteration cost of CRISP. What this paper does not resolve 

Six open questions remain (see Section 11 for the long form): a sharp worst-case characterisation of the adversarial μ directions; a sharp interior γ⋆(p) as a function of sweep count; a Bayesian interpretation of the shrunk target Pγ ; a precise correspondence between our Sharpe-optimal γ

# C Sum-normalised recursive MVO and the sign-flip pathology 

This appendix documents, as a cautionary negative result, the first hierarchical signal-aware variant we tried in the development of the tree methods of Section 4. The construction — a recursive mean–variance tree pass that normalises each node’s pair of child budgets to sum to one — is the obvious generalisation of López de Prado [2016] HRP to a signed μ, and for that reason it is the obvious trap. We refer to it throughout as Method A1 or sum-normalised recursive MVO ,matching the codebase name method_a1_weights . The conclusion reached in this appendix is straightforward: Method A1 is structurally broken as a portfolio construction, its output direction bears no fixed relationship to the oracle Markowitz portfolio w⋆ = Σ−1μ, and no choice of γ,sample size, or covariance estimator repairs it. The appendix is included because the form of the failure — a cumulative sign flip that sign-invariant direction metrics fail to report — is a useful worked illustration of two methodological points: (i) sign-invariant error metrics must be paired with sign-sensitive diagnostics, and (ii) any hierarchical method for signal-dependent allocation must carry sign information in a form the recursive 2 × 2 normaliser cannot invert. HRP-μ (Section 4.3) satisfies (ii) via leaf-level signed inverse-variance representatives; HRP-Σμ (Section 4.4) satisfies it via L1 normalisation. C.2 The sign-flip mechanism 

The sign pathology lives entirely in the denominator αraw  

> L

+ αraw  

> R

. When μ ≥ 0, the raw budgets are non-negative and their sum is positive, so (57) is the ordinary convex-combination step of HRP. When μ has mixed signs — the generic case for any signal built from alphas, momentum residuals, or factor tilts — the representative signals (sL, s R) entering the node system can be positive, negative, or near zero. The 2 × 2 solve then produces raw budgets of arbitrary signs, and their algebraic sum has no sign guarantee whatsoever. Whenever that sum is negative, division by it flips both signs in the child pair (αL, α R) simultaneously .The flip is local and it is cumulative. Across the log 2 N levels of the dendrogram, the sign pattern of the root output is the product of the O(log N ) level-wise denominator signs, none of which carry any fixed relationship to sign (Σ−1μ)i at the leaves. A sign-carrying linear object — w⋆i is linear in 

μ — cannot be recovered from a chain of sum-to-one divisions whose denominators are themselves signed. That is the whole story; the remainder of this appendix is empirical corroboration.

C.6 Why the direction-error metric does not catch this 

A reader who has only consulted the dir (w) = sin 2 θ(w, w ⋆) panels of Section 10 would not know Method A1 is broken. The direction error is sign-invariant : it measures the squared sine of the angle between w and w⋆ and therefore cannot distinguish a portfolio with cos = +0 .85 from one with cos = −0.85 . Both yield dir = 1 − 0.85 2 ≈ 0.28 . A1 populates the latter case roughly half the time, and dir aggregates the two cases into a single superficially-respectable number of about 0.28 

on the Panel A2 diagnostic of Section 10.2.3 — exactly while the method is producing the wrong sign on half the trials. This is the cleanest illustration of a general methodological point. A metric that collapses ±w

onto the same equivalence class is appropriate only for long-only or inherently sign-fixed allocators, and it will silently reward methods whose signs are uncorrelated with sign (Σ−1μ)i. For any signal-aware construction the operational metric must be the signed cosine cos (w, w ⋆) or equivalently the fraction of trials in the wrong half-space; Tables 27–28 and Figure 8 are the first places in this paper where that distinction is load-bearing. The lesson generalises: sign-invariant metrics must always be paired with a sign-sensitive diagnostic before a method is declared acceptable. 

C.7 Conclusion 

Method A1 — sum-normalised recursive MVO — should not be used for portfolio construction. Its failure is not a tuning problem and not a noise problem; it is a structural incompatibility between a sum-to-one normalisation step and a signal that can take either sign at each node. The appendix illustrates two general lessons. First, the sign-invariance trap: the direction-error metric 

dir (w) reports an acceptable number ( ≈ 0.28 ) on Method A1 exactly while A1 is antiparallel to 

w⋆ half the time; sign-invariant metrics must be paired with sign-sensitive diagnostics, without exception. Second, the design constraint for hierarchical signal-aware allocation: such a method must carry sign information through the recursion in a form the 2 × 2 normaliser cannot invert. HRP-μ (Section 4.3) satisfies this by using signed inverse-variance representatives at the leaves, which enter the node-level solve additively and are not subject to recursive sum-normalisation. HRP-Σμ (Section 4.4) satisfies it by replacing sum-to-one with L1 normalisation, which preserves all node-level signs. The sum-normalised variant is retained in the codebase as method_a1_weights 

only as a reproducible counterexample against which future recursive signal-aware schemes can be compared.

E.2 Sensitivity to ridge regularisation 

All three preconditioned methods add a small ridge λI to ̂ Σ before inversion or Gauss–Seidel sweeps. Table 31 sweeps λ ∈ { 10 −8, 10 −6, 10 −4, 10 −2} on the base universe with T = 240 and oracle μ. The CRISP curves are flat within rounding over [10 −8, 10 −4] and drop only at λ = 10 −2, where the ridge becomes comparable to the diagonal of ̂ Σ and acts as real shrinkage instead of a numerical floor. HRP-μ is equally insensitive in the interior. The interpretation is that the ridge serves only to guarantee solvability of the inverse; it is not a second shrinkage knob sitting behind γ.

E.3 Sensitivity to tree construction 

CRISP is tree-free: it never instantiates a dendrogram, so the linkage row in Table 32 is exactly constant. The row is listed for the record. HRP-μ shows modest variation of roughly 0.03 in OOS Sharpe across linkages — a reflection of the induced block structure. De Prado HRP, which discards 

μ entirely and is included as a μ-blind benchmark, is substantially more brittle: single linkage yields a negative OOS Sharpe, consistent with the chaining pathology noted in López de Prado [2016]. The gap between the tree-free and tree-based methods widens precisely when the tree is worst.
