## Abstract 

We study model-free reinforcement learning (RL) in non-stationary finite-horizon episodic Markov decision processes (MDPs) without prior knowl-edge of the non-stationarity. We focus on the piecewise-stationary (PS) setting, where both the reward and transition dynamics can change an ar-bitrary number of times. We propose Detection Augmented Reinforcement Learning (DARLING), a modular wrapper for PS-RL that applies to both tabular and linear MDPs, without knowledge of the changes. Under certain change-point separa-tion and reachability conditions, DARLING im-proves the best available dynamic regret bounds in both settings and yields strong empirical per-formance. We further establish the first mini-max lower bounds for PS-RL in tabular and lin-ear MDPs, showing that DARLING is the first nearly optimal algorithm. Experiments on stan-dard benchmarks demonstrate that DARLING consistently surpasses the state-of-the-art meth-ods across diverse non-stationary scenarios. 

## 1. Introduction 

Reinforcement Learning (RL) studies sequential decision making in unknown environments, typically modeled as Markov decision processes (MDPs), with the goal of max-imizing cumulative reward (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Most RL algorithms assume a stationary environment, where rewards and transition dynamics are fixed but unknown, im-plying a time-invariant optimal policy. In many real-world applications, however, this assumption is violated: envi-ronments evolve due to changing populations, markets, or physical conditions. Such non-stationarity is central to ap-plications including clinical treatment planning (Shortreed et al., 2011), real-time bidding (Cai et al., 2017), inven-

In these settings, stationary guarantees no longer apply and performance can degrade significantly (Ortner et al., 2020), motivating the development of reliable algorithms for non-stationary (NS) RL. Non-stationarity is often divided into two regimes: drift-ing non-stationarity, where the MDP evolves gradually, and 

abrupt non-stationarity, where the environment changes at discrete times. The latter is captured by the piecewise stationary (PS) model, in which the MDP remains station-ary on contiguous segments separated by change-points. While drifting models have received substantial attention (Ortner et al., 2019; Cheung et al., 2020; Touati & Vin-cent, 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2022; Domingues et al., 2021b), the PS setting remains comparatively under-explored in RL (Gajane et al., 2018). At the same time, recent results in the NS bandit literature indicate that algo-rithms designed for PS can be empirically robust even under drift and on experiments that deviate from strict piecewise stationarity, outperforming approaches explicitly tuned for drifting settings (Gerogiannis et al., 2025a). This suggests that the PS model can yield effective methods beyond the nominal regime of validity. Existing NS-RL algorithms differ along three key dimen-sions: (i) prior knowledge of the non-stationarity (e.g., change frequency or variation budgets), (ii) the adaptation mechanism used to respond to changes, and (iii) whether the method is model-based or model-free . Model-based approaches attempt to track the underlying dynamics as the environment evolves; while theoretically appealing, they can incur substantial computational and memory overhead and may degrade under drift due to model mis-specification and estimation error (Cheung et al., 2020; Mao et al., 2022). To this end, we focus on model-free methods. Within NS-RL, the dominant design axis is the adaptation mechanism, which yields three widely used paradigms: (i) discounted/sliding window methods (Gajane et al., 2018; Cheung et al., 2020; Touati & Vincent, 2021; Domingues et al., 2021b), (ii) budget-restart methods (Jaksch et al., 2010; Ortner et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022), and (iii) detection-restart methods (Wei & Luo, 2021). These paradigms are prevalent in the NS multi-armed bandit literature, which has served as a canonical testbed for studying non-stationarity in online learning (Garivier & Moulines, 2011; Besbes et al., 2014; Besson et al., 2022). An additional discussion on the NS bandit literature is given in the Appendix. Among these paradigms, detection-restart methods are dis-tinctive in enabling prior-free design with optimal guaran-tees: they do not require knowledge of the timing, frequency, or magnitude of changes, in contrast to discounted, sliding-window, and budget-restart methods whose performance depends on tuned parameters that encode such prior informa-tion. Recent results further suggest that restarting strategies can enjoy more favorable worst-case complexity guarantees than fully adaptive schemes (Peng & Papadimitriou, 2024). Despite the appeal of prior-free , model-free detection– restart, a theory–practice gap remains. To our knowledge, MASTER (Wei & Luo, 2021) is the only algorithm in this class with order-optimal guarantees, yet recent empirical work shows that its internal detection can be practically un-reliable, leading to performance far worse than competing alternatives (Gerogiannis et al., 2025b;a). On the theory side, to our knowledge, minimax lower bounds for PS episodic MDPs have been unavailable, obscuring the difficulty of PS-RL and the optimality landscape. These gaps motivate new PS-RL methods that are simultaneously prior-free, the-oretically grounded, and empirically robust. 

Contributions We propose DARLING, a modular and prior-free detection-restart framework for PS episodic MDPs. DARLING combines effective mean-shift detection ideas (Besson et al., 2022; Gerogiannis et al., 2025a; Huang et al., 2025b) with two tests tailored to identify changes in the reward functions and transition dynamics, and can augment 

any base RL algorithm with order-optimal stationary re-gret to lift its guarantees to the PS setting. We instantiate DARLING for tabular and linear MDPs, obtaining unified prior-free algorithms with near-optimal dynamic regret un-der piecewise stationarity. We also derive the first , to our knowledge, minimax dynamic-regret lower bounds for PS episodic MDPs in both regimes, and show that under reacha-bility and change-point separation conditions our algorithms are nearly-optimal , improving upon the best known prior-free guarantees. Finally, we evaluate extensively on PS and drifting benchmarks against state-of-the-art prior-free and prior-based baselines, where DARLING consistently out-performs existing methods and remains robust beyond its nominal regime.

## 3. Our Algorithm 

What to Detect A key challenge in prior-free detection-restart design is deciding what signal to monitor so that changes are detected quickly in realistic sample regimes. The only prior-free algorithm with order-optimal guarantees in our setting, MASTER, detects non-stationarity only in-directly by checking whether certain stationary-regret guar-antees are violated. While this principle enables theoretical guarantees, it can be overly conservative in practice, requir-ing unrealistic number of episodes to detect changes even 3Detection Augmented Reinforcement Learning with Non-Stationary Guarantees 

in favorable instances (Gerogiannis et al., 2025b), which in turn leads to infeasible restarts and poor empirical per-formance. DARLING takes a different approach: it detects non-stationarity directly by monitoring mean shifts in quan-tities that characterize the MDP itself . The environment in each episode t is fully specified by {rth, P th}Hh=1 ; therefore, any change must manifest as a change in at least one of these objects. Thus, we run two dedicated tests: one targeting changes in rewards and one targeting changes in transitions. This “detect-the-model” viewpoint yields interpretable de-tection statistics and avoids relying on regret-violation. 

How to Detect Monitoring changes in {rth, P th}Hh=1 di-rectly is challenging because informative triples (s, a, h )

may be infinite and most may be rarely visited under the behavior induced by the base learner or optimal policy. DARLING addresses this by (i) restricting attention to a finite probe collection P ⊆ S × A × [H] constructed from step-dependent exploration supports {S e,h }h∈[H] ⊆ S 

and {A s

Probing episodes and freezing the base learner Within segment k (after the last restart at episode τ ), DARLING designates one probing episode every ⌈1/α k⌉ episodes. In a probing episode, the agent may query the base learner L

for its suggested actions, but does not update L. At step

Freezing L preserves modularity: the stationary regret guarantees of L generally rely on its own (on-policy) interaction stream, while probing intervenes on the visitation distribution and can corrupt both theory and practice if used for updates. 

Detection histories and monitored streams DARLING performs change detection by maintaining scalar histories for the probe triples (s, a, h ) ∈ P , and applying the same univariate mean-shift detector to each history. Concretely, whenever a probing episode visits an exploration state sth ∈Se,h and takes a probe action ath = a ∈ A sth

Reward histories (Test 1). For each probed triple (s, a, h ),we append the scalar reward sample to H(r)(s,a,h ), whose mean equals the probed reward rth(s, a ).

Transition histories (Test 2). To detect changes in P th(· | 

s, a ), we monitor shifts in expected successor features . For a reference action a′ ∈ A define the successor-feature vector

Since our detector is univariate, we append each coordi-nate [X(t;s,a,h,a ′)]j to the scalar history H(P )(s,a,h,j,a ′) for all 

(j, a ′) ∈ [d] × A . Thus, DARLING reduces PS-RL change detection to detecting mean shifts across a finite collec-tion of scalar streams indexed by (s, a, h ) (rewards) and 

(s, a, h, j, a ′) (successor-feature coordinates). The mean of the successor-feature is linear in ϕ(s, a ); hence any change in the underlying transition parameters that is visible on the probe span induces a mean shift in at least one successor-feature coordinate stream (formalized in Section 4.1). 

The Detector DARLING uses the univariate mean-shift detector D in Algorithm 2. Given a history H =

{X1, . . . , X n}, the generalized detection test (GDT) checks whether the sequence exhibits a mean shift at any candi-date split point. Specifically, for each k ∈ { 1, . . . , n − 1},it forms empirical means on the two segments, ˆμ1: k and

If GTS k exceeds a threshold βGDT (n, δ F), the test indicates a detected change. The threshold is calibrated to control false alarms uniformly over time at level δF, while 

δD parameterizes the desired detection reliability. Algorith-mically, every time DARLING appends a new observation to a history, it runs D on the updated history. 4.5. Lower Bounds 

We provide information-theoretic lower bounds of the dy-namic regret to characterize the fundamental limits in PS-RL in finite-horizon tabular and linear MDPs. 

Theorem 4.12. For any algorithm, there exists an episodic, finite-horizon, tabular PS-MDP such that the dynamic regret of the algorithm is at least Ω( √SAH 3NT T ).Proof Sketch We construct a family of 2NT +1 hard instances indexed by binary vectors, where each MDP contains a waiting state, a root, an A-ary tree with AD−1 leaves, and absorbing states. In each stationary segment k, the optimal state-action-step triple depends adversarially on the k-th index bit: when ik = 0 it is fixed, while when ik = 1 it is the least-visited triple under any policy, forcing exploration or high regret. We lower bound regret on paired instances differing in one bit using the Bretagnolle–Huber inequality, relating it to KL divergence controlled by visit counts to the least-visited triple. Optimizing the bias parameter and averaging over all instances yields the lower bound. The full proof of Theorem 4.12 is given in the Appendix. 

Theorem 4.13. For any algorithm, there exists an episodic, finite-horizon, linear PS-MDP such that the dynamic regret of the algorithm is at least Ω( d√H3NT T ).Proof Sketch We follow the linear-mixture MDP hard in-stance and reduction of Zhou et al. (2021). We construct a piecewise-stationary MDP with NT +1 segments, where within each segment the value gap in one episode lower bounds (up to constants) the sum of H/ 2 independent Bernoulli linear-bandit gaps. Applying the Bernoulli linear-bandit lower bound in each segment and stage yields per-segment regret and summing over segments leads to the final result. The proof of this theorem is provided in the Appendix. Hence it is evident that according to Theorems 4.12,4.13, DARLING is nearly-optimal under Assumptions 4.7, 4.10.

Over-all, these results suggest that DARLING narrows the theory– practice gap in prior-free PS-RL, combining near-optimal guarantees with consistently strong empirical performance across diverse non-stationary settings. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We introduced DARLING, a modular, prior-free detection– restart framework for piecewise-stationary (PS) episodic RL. Unlike prior-free baselines that detect changes indi-rectly via regret-violation, DARLING detects the model 

by monitoring mean shifts in probed reward streams and successor-feature streams, and can wrap any stationary RL algorithm with optimal regret, while preserving its on-policy learning guarantees via probing episodes that freeze the base learner. Under identifiability and reachability conditions, and with a broad class of logarithmic-sample-complexity detectors, DARLING attains near-minimax dynamic regret in both tabular and linear MDPs. We also establish, to our knowledge, the first minimax dynamic-regret lower bounds for PS episodic MDPs, showing our rates are nearly op-timal. Empirically, DARLING consistently outperforms state-of-the-art prior-free and tuned restart baselines on exploration-hard PS benchmarks and remains robust under drifting non-stationarity, while retaining practical runtime. An interesting direction, would be to adapt the detection mechanism or propose a novel detector to handle uncount-ably infinite spaces. Finally, future work also includes the extension of DARLING to infinite-horizon MDPs. 8Detection Augmented Reinforcement Learning with Non-Stationary Guarantees

## A. Related Work in Non-Stationary Bandits 

Non-stationary (NS) bandits are a canonical testbed for studying learning under distribution shift, and they have strongly influenced how NS-RL algorithms are designed and analyzed. A useful way to organize the NS bandit literature is along two largely orthogonal axes: (i) the adaptation mechanism —adaptive methods that continuously emphasize recency versus restarting methods that explicitly reset the learner—and (ii) the extent of prior knowledge required about the non-stationarity— prior-based (requiring tuned parameters linked to variation/breakpoints) versus prior-free (not requiring such tuning). This taxonomy parallels the dominant paradigms in NS-RL (discounting/windowing, budget-restart, detection-restart), and helps clarify which assumptions are needed to obtain guarantees and practical performance (Garivier & Moulines, 2011; Besbes et al., 2014; Besson et al., 2022). 

Adaptive methods: discounting and sliding windows. Adaptive approaches track change by continuously down-weighting or discarding older samples, typically via exponential discounting or fixed-length sliding windows. These methods are conceptually simple and widely applicable, but their performance depends on selecting a discount factor or window length that matches the (unknown) timescale of non-stationarity, rendering them typically prior-based . In NS-MABs, classical examples include discounted UCB and sliding-window UCB (Kocsis & Szepesv ´ari, 2006; Garivier & Moulines, 2011). This paradigm has been extended to structured bandits, including NS linear bandits (NS-LBs) (Cheung et al., 2019; Russac et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023), NS generalized linear bandits (NS-GLBs) (Faury et al., 2021; Russac et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2023), and NS self-concordant bandits (NS-SCBs) (Russac et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). Analogous ideas also appear in non-parametric settings such as kernelized bandits (NS-KBs), where recency-weighted or windowed estimators are combined with optimism (Deng et al., 2022; Zhou & Shroff, 2021). Overall, discounting/windowing provides a general-purpose route to adaptivity, but introduces a non-trivial tuning problem: too much forgetting increases variance, while too little forgetting yields bias under shift. 

Restarting methods: budgeted restarts. A second family of approaches explicitly restarts the learning process, typically on a schedule designed to control the amount of stale data. In NS bandits, the most common restarting template is the budget-restart strategy, which restarts at predetermined times (or on epochs of increasing lengths) selected using a variation/breakpoint budget. This yields strong theoretical guarantees when the budget is known or can be tuned, but again is usually prior-based . Representative results include the classical NS-MAB framework in Besbes et al. (2014), as well as extensions to structured settings such as NS-LBs/NS-GLBs (Zhao et al., 2020) and NS-KBs (Zhou & Shroff, 2021). Conceptually, budget-restart trades off two error sources: within-epoch learning (stationary regret) and cross-epoch mismatch (stale data), and the schedule is tuned to balance these terms. 

Restarting methods: detection-based restarts. Detection-restart methods aim to remove the explicit dependence on a known non-stationarity budget by testing for change and restarting only when evidence accumulates. This is particularly natural in abrupt (piecewise-stationary) models, where changes are sparse but impactful. In NS-MABs, prior-based detection-restart methods include algorithms that rely on thresholds calibrated to the change budget or minimal gap assumptions (Liu et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2019). More recent prior-free approaches emphasize modular change detection primitives (e.g., GLR/CuSum-type tests) coupled with bandit exploration policies, enabling guarantees without knowing the number/timing of changes (Auer et al., 2019; Besson et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2025a). Beyond MABs, related detection-restart ideas have been developed for richer structured classes, including NS linear and kernelized bandits (Hong et al., 2023) and NS contextual bandits (NS-CBs) (Luo et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2019). At a high level, these methods separate concerns: a base algorithm drives exploration/exploitation within a segment, while a statistical test monitors for distributional shifts and triggers a reset. Importantly, in the detection-based restart literature there exist two black-box, prior-free methodologies which are applicable to all the general bandit settings DAL (Gerogiannis et al., 2025a) and MASTER (Wei & Luo, 2021).

C.2. Experimental Environments Exploration-hard “combination-lock” benchmarks. We evaluate on two standard combination-lock MDPs (Mao et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022) which are designed to require deep exploration : a near-optimal return is only obtained by executing a long, specific action prefix of length H, while most other action sequences quickly lead to a “trap” region with small but dense rewards. This creates a strong local optimum (exploiting the trap) that can dominate na ˜A¯ve exploration, even though the globally optimal behavior is to keep searching for the rare high-reward chain. In particular, under uniform exploration the probability of reaching the high-reward endpoint scales as A−H , so identifying the correct action sequence can be exponentially hard in H without principled exploration or structure. We use these benchmarks to stress-test adaptation under both PS and drifting non-stationarity. 

Tabular MDP: Bidirectional Diabolical Combination Lock. We follow the Bidirectional Diabolical Combination Lock construction Mao et al. (2022) in which each episode starts from a fixed initial state. The first action routes the agent into one of two “locks” (paths), each a chain of length H; along each chain, at every step there is a unique correct action that advances to the next state on that path, whereas any of the other A − 1 actions sends the agent to an absorbing sinking state. The MDP is mildly stochastic: even when the agent selects the correct action, the intended transition succeeds with probability 0.98 , and with probability 0.02 the agent still falls into the sink. Rewards are sparse on the optimal behavior: taking correct actions yields reward 0, and the only large reward occurs at the endpoint of a path (one endpoint gives reward 

1, the other gives 0.25 ). By contrast, entering the sink yields a small reward of 18H at the transition step and then a per-step reward of 18H thereafter, making the sink a tempting locally-optimal attractor. Non-stationarity. For drifting experiments we use the original gradual protocol in Mao et al. (2022): the routing dynamics at the initial state are linearly morphed over time so that an action that initially reaches path 1 with probability 0.98 (and path 2 with probability 0.02 ) is gradually transformed to reach path 1 with probability 0.02 (and path 2 with probability 0.98 ), with the symmetric change applied to the other action. For abrupt PS experiments, we use the same endpoint-swap mechanism as Mao et al. (2022), but replace their fixed-period switching with a geometric change-point model Gerogiannis et al. (2025b): segment lengths are i.i.d. geometric with parameter T −ξ for ξ ∈ { 0.4, 0.6, 0.8} over a horizon of T = 50000 episodes, and at each change-point the two endpoints swap identities (the 1 and 0.25 rewards exchange). Unless otherwise stated, we use the benchmark parameterization (Mao et al., 2022) H = 5 , S = 10 , A = 2 , and report cumulative reward averaged over multiple random seeds.
