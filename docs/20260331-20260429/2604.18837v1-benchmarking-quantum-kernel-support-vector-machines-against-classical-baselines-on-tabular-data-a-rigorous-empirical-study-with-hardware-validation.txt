Abstract 

Quantum kernel methods have been proposed as a promising approach for leveraging near-term quantum computers for supervised learning, yet rigorous benchmarks against strong classical baselines remain scarce. We present a comprehensive empirical study of quantum kernel support vector machines (QSVMs) across nine binary classification datasets, four quantum feature maps, three classical kernels, and multiple noise models, totalling 970 experiments with strict nested cross-validation. Our analysis spans four phases: (i) statistical significance testing, revealing that none of 29 pairwise quantum–classical comparisons reach significance at α = 0 .05; (ii) learning curve analysis over six training fractions, showing steeper quantum slopes on six of eight datasets that nonetheless fail to close the gap to the best classical baseline; (iii) hardware validation on IBM ibm fez (Heron r2), demonstrating kernel fidelity r ≥ 0.976 across six experiments; and (iv) seed sensitivity analysis confirming reproducibility (mean CV 1.4%). A Kruskal–Wallis factorial analysis reveals that dataset choice dominates performance variance ( ε2 = 0 .73), while kernel type accounts for only 9%. Spectral analysis offers a mechanistic explanation: current quantum feature maps produce eigenspectra that are either too flat or too concentrated, missing the intermediate profile of the best classical kernel, the radial basis function (RBF). Quantum kernel training (QKT) via kernel-target alignment yields the single competitive result – balanced accuracy 0.968 on breast cancer – but with 

∼ 2,000 × computational overhead. Our findings provide actionable guidelines for quantum kernel research. The complete benchmark suite is publicly available to facilitate reproduction and extension. 

Keywords: Quantum machine learning, Quantum kernel methods, Support vector ma-chines, Benchmarking, Hardware validation, Feature maps

2.2 Quantum Kernel Methods 

Quantum kernel methods [Havl´ ıˇ cek et al., 2019, Schuld and Killoran, 2019] replace the classical feature map ϕ with a quantum feature map U (x) that encodes classical data into quantum states: 

|ψ(x)⟩ = U (x)|0⟩⊗n, (3) where U (x) is a parameterised unitary acting on n qubits, and |0⟩⊗n is the all-zero initial state. The quantum kernel is then defined as the fidelity between encoded states: 

kQ(x, z) = |⟨ ψ(x)|ψ(z)⟩| 2 = |⟨ 0|⊗nU †(x)U (z)|0⟩⊗n|2. (4) This fidelity kernel is symmetric, bounded in [0 , 1], satisfies kQ(x, x) = 1, and is PSD by con-struction [Schuld, 2021], since kQ(x, z) = |⟨ ψ(x)|ψ(z)⟩| 2 = Tr( ρxρz ) where ρx = |ψ(x)⟩⟨ ψ(x)|.3On a quantum computer, kQ(x, z) is estimated via the compute-uncompute circuit: apply U (x), then U †(z), and measure the probability of the all-zero bitstring. The implicit quantum feature map ϕQ(x) = |ψ(x)⟩ embeds data into a 2 n-dimensional Hilbert space. For n qubits, the feature space dimension grows exponentially, which may pro-vide representational power beyond efficient classical simulation [Havl´ ıˇ cek et al., 2019]. How-ever, Schuld [Schuld, 2021] proved that all quantum models with a fixed encoding circuit are mathematically equivalent to classical kernel methods, implying that any potential advantage must come from the specific structure of the quantum feature map rather than from quantum computation per se.

# 3 Related Work 

Theoretical landscapes The theoretical underpinnings of quantum kernel methods were established by Havl´ ıˇ cek et al. [Havl´ ıˇ cek et al., 2019], who demonstrated that quantum com-puters can generate kernel functions corresponding to classically intractable feature maps, and by Schuld and Killoran [Schuld and Killoran, 2019], who formalised the connection between quantum circuits and kernel methods. Liu et al. [Liu et al., 2021] proved a rigorous quantum speed-up for a carefully constructed classification problem, establishing that provable advan-tages exist in principle. However, Huang et al. [Huang et al., 2021] showed that the advantage depends critically on the relationship between the quantum feature map and the data distribu-tion – classical methods equipped with a prediction function derived from the quantum model can match its performance when classical data is abundant. 

Concentration and trainability K¨ ubler et al. [K¨ ubler et al., 2021] analysed the inductive bias of quantum kernels, demonstrating that random quantum feature maps tend to produce kernels that are essentially random inner products in high-dimensional spaces, offering no useful structure for classification. Thanasilp et al. [Thanasilp et al., 2024] proved that quantum kernels exhibit exponential concentration : as the number of qubits grows, all kernel values concentrate around a fixed value, rendering the kernel matrix uninformative. # 4 Methodology 

4.1 Datasets 

We evaluate on nine binary classification datasets from the UCI Machine Learning Reposi-tory [Dua and Graff, 2017] and OpenML [Vanschoren et al., 2014], selected to span a range of sample sizes ( N ∈ [208 , 4601]), dimensionalities ( d ∈ [3 , 60]), and class balances. Table 2 sum-marises the dataset characteristics. While the majority of datasets originate from biomedical and physical-science domains, they cover distinct statistical profiles (feature counts, class ra-tios, separability) and are standard benchmarks in the classical SVM literature, enabling direct comparison with established baselines. 

4.2 Preprocessing and Dimensionality Reduction 

Quantum feature maps require low-dimensional input, because embedding data into an exces-sively large Hilbert space often results in exponentially vanishing inner products, rendering the quantum kernel uninformative as the Gram matrix converges toward the identity matrix. This necessitates dimensionality reduction from the original feature space to k ∈ { 3, 4, 6, 8, 10 , 12 , 14 }

dimensions. The number of qubits depends on the encoding strategy: feature maps with double-feature encoding (Rot2DoF, Belis, Sakhnenko10) embed two features per qubit via paired single-qubit rotations, requiring n = ⌈k/ 2⌉ qubits for k features; ZZFeatureMap uses single-feature encoding with one qubit per feature, requiring n = k qubits. We employ three reduction strate-gies: 

• PCA (Principal Component Analysis): An unsupervised linear projection that retains the top-k orthogonal principal components maximising explained variance [Pedregosa et al., 2011], preceded by standard scaling to zero mean and unit variance. 

• NMF (Non-negative Matrix Factorisation) [Lee and Seung, 1999]: A parts-based decom-position that factorises the feature matrix into k non-negative components, preserving additive structure. Preceded by min-max scaling to [0 , 1] to enforce the non-negativity constraint. 7• Tree (SelectFromModel): A supervised feature-selection method that ranks the original features by Gini importance from a fitted DecisionTreeClassifier and selects the top-k

features, preceded by standard scaling. Unlike PCA and NMF, this retains interpretable original features rather than constructing linear combinations. All preprocessing – scaling and dimensionality reduction – is applied per outer CV fold :the scaler and reducer are fit exclusively on the training split and then used to transform both training and test splits. This prevents information leakage from test-fold statistics into the feature transforms. For quantum experiments, the per-fold design requires computing a separate kernel matrix for each outer fold (rather than a single global N × N matrix), increasing the kernel budget by a factor equal to the number of outer folds. We accept this additional cost to ensure that absolute performance estimates are unbiased. Reduced features are passed directly to the quantum feature-map circuits as rotation-gate angles (no further rescaling to a fixed range such as [0 , π ] is applied).

4.4 Kernel Computation 

We compute kernel matrices via three pathways: 

Ideal (statevector) Exact overlap kQ(x, z) = |⟨ ψ(x)|ψ(z)⟩| 2 computed via statevector sim-ulation. Each data point is embedded once: the feature-map circuit U (x) is applied to |0⟩⊗n

and the resulting 2 n-dimensional statevector is stored. Kernel entries are then computed as classical inner products |⟨ ψ(x)|ψ(z)⟩| 2, requiring O(N ) circuit simulations plus O(N 2) classical dot products for an N -sample dataset. This provides the noise-free reference kernel matrix; diagonal entries are 1 by definition. 

Noisy (density matrix) The kernel is computed as the Hilbert–Schmidt inner product 

kQ(x, z) = Tr[ ρ(x) · ρ(z)], where ρ(x) is the density matrix of the encoded state U (x)|0⟩⊗n

evolved under a depolarising noise model with single-qubit error rate p1q = 10 −3 and two-qubit error rate p2q = 10 −2, representative of current superconducting hardware. For pure states this equals the fidelity |⟨ ψ(x)|ψ(z)⟩| 2; for the mixed states arising from decoherence the Hilbert– Schmidt inner product remains a valid positive semi-definite kernel but is no longer identical to the Uhlmann fidelity [Havl´ ıˇ cek et al., 2019]. The density-matrix trace formulation avoids the O(N 2) circuit overhead of the compute-uncompute approach: each data point requires only 

one circuit simulation to obtain ρ(x), and kernel entries are computed as classical matrix inner products. This provides an O(N ) circuit advantage while faithfully modelling incoherent noise. 

Hardware (IBM QPU) Fidelity-based compute-uncompute circuits executed on IBM ibm fez (Heron r2, 156 qubits) [IBM Quantum, 2024] using Qiskit Runtime SamplerV2. For each pair (x, z), the circuit U (x) followed by U †(z) is applied and measured. Circuits are transpiled at 

optimization level=3 to the native gate set {rz, sx, controlled-Z (CZ) , x }, minimising circuit depth. Each kernel entry is estimated from 1 024 shots as the empirical probability of the all-zero bitstring: ˆk(x, z) = count( 0)/1024. No readout error mitigation is applied; results therefore reflect raw hardware fidelity. 

Kernel post-processing Simulation kernels (ideal and noisy) are positive semi-definite (PSD) by construction: ideal kernels are Gram matrices of statevector inner products, and noisy ker-nels are Hilbert–Schmidt inner products of positive operators. No explicit eigenvalue clipping is applied. Hardware kernel matrices, which can be slightly indefinite due to shot noise, are 9also used without PSD projection: scikit-learn’s SVC accepts pre-computed kernels that may be indefinite, and retaining the raw matrices preserves the unfiltered hardware signal for fidelity analysis. 

4.5 Classical Baselines 

We compare against three classical SVM kernels: 

• Linear : k(x, z) = x⊤z.

• RBF : k(x, z) = exp( −γ∥x − z∥2) with γ = 1 /(d · Var( X)) (scikit-learn gamma="scale" ). 

• Polynomial (degree 3): k(x, z) = ( γx⊤z)3, i.e., the homogeneous variant ( r = 0 in the general form of Eq. 2’s discussion; scikit-learn SVC default coef0=0 ). Classical kernels are computed analytically using scikit-learn [Pedregosa et al., 2011], providing exact baselines without shot noise or hardware artefacts. 

4.6 Evaluation Protocol 

Nested cross-validation All experiments use stratified nested cross-validation [Cawley and Talbot, 2010, Varma and Simon, 2006]: the main benchmark (V1) uses a 5 × 3 design (5 outer, 3 inner folds) and the extended study (V2) uses 5 × 5 (5 outer, 5 inner folds). In both cases, the outer folds provide unbiased performance estimation and the inner folds select the C-hyperparameter from C ∈ { 0.01 , 0.1, 1, 10 , 100 }. For the Parkinson 489 dataset, group-aware splits ensure no speaker appears in both training and test folds. For each outer fold, prepro-cessing is fit on the training split, the training and test features are transformed, and the kernel matrices are computed from the transformed features. Inner CV operates on the training-fold kernel sub-matrix.

Statistical testing We employ nonparametric rank-based tests, which make no distributional assumptions about the per-fold accuracy values: 

• Wilcoxon signed-rank test [Wilcoxon, 1945]: Two-sided paired comparison of per-fold BA arrays ( n = 5) between quantum and classical kernels. This test is the nonparametric counterpart of the paired t-test and is appropriate for small paired samples. 

• Friedman test [Friedman, 1937]: Omnibus test for differences among multiple methods within each dataset, the nonparametric equivalent of repeated-measures ANOVA. When significant, Nemenyi post-hoc tests identify which pairs of methods differ. 10 The Wilcoxon signed-rank and Friedman tests are the recommended nonparametric methods for classifier comparison across multiple datasets [Demˇ sar, 2006]. In addition, we perform a factorial screening via: 

• Kruskal–Wallis H-test [Kruskal and Wallis, 1952]: One-way analysis of seven experimental factors (dataset, reducer, k, kernel family, backend, QKT flag, group) on balanced accu-racy across all 222 extended-study configurations, with epsilon-squared ( ε2) effect sizes. Unlike the paired tests above, Kruskal–Wallis treats the experimental configurations as independent observations and identifies which design factors most influence performance – the nonparametric equivalent of one-way ANOVA. Within the Phase 4 seed sensitivity analysis, Bonferroni correction is applied across the seven per-dataset comparisons ( αcorr = 0 .05 /7). 

Reproducibility Deterministic seeding (seed = 42) is used throughout. All experiments log the Python environment, library versions, git commit hash, and random states. Kernel matrices are cached as content-addressable NumPy compressed archive ( .npz ) files (xxhash-based keys) for exact reproducibility. 

# 5 Experimental Setup 

The benchmark is organised into two experimental campaigns – a main benchmark and a targeted extended study – plus four analysis phases, totalling 970 kernel evaluations and 8 400 additional SVM fits. 

5.1 Main Benchmark (V1) 

This campaign comprises 748 experiments across five systematic sweeps: 

• Main comparison: 8 datasets × 7 kernels (4 quantum + linear + rbf + poly) at default settings ( k = dmax , reps = 2, no noise). 

• Reducer ablation: PCA vs. NMF vs. tree-based selection on representative dataset– kernel pairs. 

• Qubit scaling: k ∈ { 3, 4, 6, 8} features with both PCA and NMF. 

• Circuit depth: reps ∈ { 1, 2, 3} for all quantum feature maps. 

• Noise sensitivity: Depolarising noise at p1q ∈ { 0, 5×10 −4, 10 −3, 5×10 −3} using density-matrix simulation. All experiments use 5 × 3 nested CV, producing 588 cached kernel matrices and 748 result records. 11 5.2 Extended Study (V2) 

Motivated by V1 findings, the extended study adds 222 targeted experiments: 

• Higher-k: k ∈ { 10 , 12 , 14 } for breast cancer, ionosphere, and sonar with NMF and tree reducers. Tests whether more qubits improve quantum kernel performance. 

• Larger datasets: spambase ( n = 1 ,000 stratified subsample) and diabetes pima at k ∈{4, 6, 8, 10 }. Tests scalability to larger problems. 

• Quantum Kernel Training (QKT): 28 QKT experiments across 7 datasets with per-fold theta optimisation via L-BFGS-B. Tests whether learnable feature scaling can close the quantum–classical gap. The extended study uses 5 × 5 nested CV (more inner folds), generating 95 additional kernel matrices. QKT experiments each require 50–170 iterations of kernel recomputation per fold, resulting in ∼ 2,000 × computational overhead vs. classical baselines. 

5.3 Analysis Phases 

The analysis phases operate on existing data from both campaigns without generating new kernel matrices: 

• Phase 1 (Statistical analysis): 29 Wilcoxon tests, 9 Friedman tests, 7-factor Kruskal– Wallis, QKT stability analysis (28 configs), kernel eigenvalue decomposition (95 matrices), computational cost analysis, and dataset suitability criterion. 

• Phase 2 (Learning curves): 720 data points from 24 representative configurations (8 datasets × 3 categories) at 6 training fractions (10%, 20%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%), using precomputed kernel sub-matrices. 

• Phase 3 (Hardware validation): 6 experiments on IBM ibm fez (Heron r2, 156 qubits) [IBM Quantum, 2024], designed to answer three research questions. Q1 (Headline fidelity): Does the hardware kernel faithfully reproduce ideal simulation? 5.4 Implementation 

The benchmark suite is implemented in Python 3.10+ using Qiskit 2.1+ [Javadi-Abhari et al., 2024] for quantum circuit construction and simulation, and scikit-learn [Pedregosa et al., 2011] for classical SVMs. The complete codebase comprises ∼18 700 non-blank lines of Python across 22 modules and 51 scripts, with 132 unit tests achieving full pass status. All quantum circuits use native-gate ( SX +RZ ) decomposition. Hardware experiments use Qiskit Runtime SamplerV2 in Job mode (IBM Quantum Open Plan, no sessions) with optimization level=3 transpilation. No readout error mitigation is applied; results reflect raw hardware fidelity. Computation was performed on a standard workstation (11th Gen Intel Core i7-1185G7, 3.00 GHz, 16 GB RAM) for simulation, and IBM ibm fez (156-qubit Heron r2 processor) for hardware experiments. Hardware wall times range from 11 to 74 minutes per experiment (dominated by queue wait times). The complete simulation benchmark requires approximately 48 hours of compute time.

# 7 Discussion 

7.1 Why Quantum Kernels Underperform: A Structural Explanation 

The spectral analysis in Sect. 6.2 reveals that current quantum feature maps produce kernel ma-trices with extreme eigenspectra – either near-identity (belis) or near-rank-1 (rot2dof) – failing to provide the intermediate spectral structure that enables effective SVM decision boundaries. The RBF kernel, by contrast, occupies a “Goldilocks zone” with moderate spectral con-centration (effective rank ratio 0.06–0.07). This is no coincidence: the RBF bandwidth γ is specifically tuned (via scikit-learn’s auto-scaling) to match the data distribution, while quan-tum feature maps encode data through a fixed nonlinear transformation without such adaptive scaling. 19 Implications for quantum feature map design This analysis suggests that the quantum kernel community should prioritise feature maps with tunable spectral properties . QKT [Hubregt-sen et al., 2022] is one approach, but it is computationally expensive ( ∼ 2,000 × overhead). Alternative strategies include: 

• Bandwidth-aware encoding : Introducing a learnable or data-dependent rescaling of fea-tures to [0 , π ] before encoding, analogous to RBF γ tuning. 

• Covariant kernels : Leveraging data symmetries to construct structurally matched feature maps [Glick et al., 2024]. 

• Spectral pre-screening : Computing the effective rank ratio of a candidate quantum kernel 

before running the full SVM pipeline, rejecting kernels with extreme profiles. 

7.2 Dataset Dominance and Benchmarking Practice 

The finding that dataset choice explains 73% of performance variance (Sect. 6.2) has important implications for the QML benchmarking community. It suggests that: 1. Performance differences between quantum feature maps on a single dataset are largely uninformative – the dataset itself, not the kernel, drives the result. 2. Future benchmarks should prioritise dataset diversity over exhaustive kernel hyperparam-eter sweeps. 3. Claims of quantum advantage based on a single or few datasets are inherently fragile, as our Phase 4 results demonstrate: the direction of the quantum–classical gap is consistent across seeds, but the dataset determines whether it favours quantum or classical. The non-linearity gap (RBF − linear BA) shows a Spearman correlation with quantum ad-vantage ( ρ = 0 .683, p = 0 .042), suggesting potential for pre-screening: datasets where classical non-linearity is minimal may be better candidates for quantum kernels, aligning with the the-oretical insight that quantum advantage should occur for problems that are hard for classical feature maps [Liu et al., 2021, Huang et al., 2021]. We note that this correlation is based on only nine datasets and is at the boundary of statistical significance; it should be interpreted as a suggestive hypothesis rather than a confirmatory result. 

7.3 Hardware Validation and the Simulation–Reality Gap 

The high kernel fidelity established in Sect. 6.4 ( r ≥ 0.976 across all experiments) provides strong evidence that the negative results from our simulation-based benchmark transfer to real hardware at the tested circuit scales (3–5 qubits, depth 22–74). The noise-as-regulariser trend observed in Sect. 6.4 (4/6 experiments, mean +1 .5 pp) aligns with theoretical work by Heyraud et al. [2022], who showed that decoherence suppresses the effective kernel rank, acting as implicit regularisation. 7.4 Quantum Kernel Training: Promise and Limitations 

QKT achieves the single competitive quantum result (BA = 0 .968 on breast cancer via belis/tree/k=8), almost matching the classical ceiling (BA = 0 .976). However, several concerns temper this suc-cess: 1. Computational cost : QKT requires 50–170 iterations per fold, each involving a full kernel recomputation, resulting in ∼ 2,000 × overhead vs. classical SVM. 2. Low convergence : Only 13.6% of QKT configurations converge (reach optimal KTA within tolerance). Most hit the iteration limit. 21 3. Theta instability : Mean coefficient of variation of QKT parameters across folds is 0.54, indicating that the optimiser finds different solutions per fold. While KTA consistently improves (mean +0.13), the structure of the learned scaling varies. 4. Dataset sensitivity : QKT improves belis on 5/7 datasets but harms performance on small datasets (sonar: −4 to −6 pp regression), suggesting overfitting of the theta param-eters. 5. Haberman failure : QKT on haberman – the sole dataset with quantum favourability – yields BA = 0 .500–0 .586 (near random), indicating that QKT cannot exploit the same structure that fixed quantum kernels capture. These findings suggest that QKT is best understood as a kernel bandwidth tuning method rather than a fundamental quantum advantage mechanism. 

7.5 Limitations 

Simulation fidelity While our hardware experiments validate simulation at 3–5 qubits, the noise landscape at higher qubit counts may differ qualitatively. Coherent errors, crosstalk, and qubit leakage (i.e., population transfer to non-computational states) – not captured by our depolarising noise model – may become significant at n > 10. 

Statistical power The Wilcoxon signed-rank test with n = 5 folds has a minimum achievable 

p-value of 0.0625, limiting power for small effect sizes. Our Phase 4 seed analysis partially addresses this, but the ideal approach would be repeated k-fold CV with more folds or bootstrap resampling. 

Preprocessing cost Our per-fold preprocessing design – fitting scalers and dimensionality reducers independently on each outer training fold – requires computing a separate kernel matrix for every outer fold rather than caching a single global N × N matrix. For quantum kernels this multiplies the already-dominant circuit budget by the number of outer folds. We accept this cost to ensure unbiased absolute performance estimates; future work on efficient per-fold kernel caching or feature-map parameter sharing may reduce this overhead. 

Binary classification only All experiments address binary classification. The findings may not generalise to multi-class problems, regression, or structured prediction tasks. 

Dataset scope Our nine datasets, while diverse, are drawn from the UCI repository and may not represent all relevant application domains. Industrial datasets with specific geometric structure may exhibit different quantum–classical dynamics. 

Feature map selection We test four quantum feature maps, all based on single- and two-qubit gates with reps ≤ 3. More expressive architectures (e.g., hardware-efficient circuit designs, problem-specific layouts) might perform differently. 22 8 Conclusion 

We have presented a comprehensive, four-phase empirical study of quantum kernel support vector machines, benchmarking four quantum feature maps against three classical SVM kernels across nine binary classification datasets, with 970 total experiments and strict nested cross-validation (main benchmark: 5 × 3; extended study: 5 × 5). 

Main findings Our results lead to the following principal conclusions: 1. No quantum advantage on standard tabular data. None of the 29 pairwise com-parisons reach statistical significance at α = 0 .05. The Phase 4 seed analysis (16 seeds, 

n = 8 ,400 SVM fits) confirms that classical kernels are significantly better on 6/7 datasets (p < 0.001). Only haberman shows robust quantum favourability ( p = 0 .004, 87.5% seed win rate), with a modest +1 .9 pp advantage. 2. Dataset choice dominates performance. Kruskal–Wallis factorial analysis reveals that dataset selection accounts for 73% of performance variance ( ε2 = 0 .73), while kernel type contributes only 9%. This implies that quantum–classical comparisons on a single dataset are insufficient. 3. Spectral mismatch explains the gap. Quantum feature maps produce kernel ma-trices with extreme eigenspectra – either near-identity (belis) or near-rank-1 (rot2dof) – while the best classical kernel (RBF) achieves an intermediate profile. This “Goldilocks hypothesis” provides a constructive design criterion for future quantum feature maps. 4. Hardware validation confirms simulation faithfulness. Across six experiments on IBM ibm fez (Heron r2), kernel fidelity satisfies r ≥ 0.976 (mean r = 0 .990), and downstream SVM performance is statistically indistinguishable from simulation. The negative results from simulation transfer to hardware. 5. QKT yields the sole competitive result, at high cost. Quantum kernel training via KTA optimisation reaches BA = 0 .968 on breast cancer, near the classical ceiling of 0.976 – but with ∼ 2,000 × computational overhead, 13.6% convergence rate, and high parameter instability. 6. Quantum kernels show steeper individual slopes but no aggregate data advan-tage. Learning curves show steeper quantum slopes on 6/8 datasets and gap narrowing on 6/8; however, the mean quantum and classical slopes are equal (0.032), indicating that while quantum kernels start from a lower baseline they learn at a comparable aggregate rate. 

Recommendations Based on these findings, we offer the following recommendations for the quantum kernel research community: 

• For benchmarking : Use nested cross-validation, multiple diverse datasets, and statistical significance testing with effect sizes. Report confidence intervals, not just point estimates. 23 • For feature map design : Target intermediate spectral profiles. Compute the effective rank ratio as a cheap diagnostic before committing to expensive evaluations. 

• For QKT : Invest in more efficient optimisation strategies (e.g., analytic gradients, quan-tum natural gradient) to reduce the ∼ 2,000 × overhead. Address convergence instability. 

• For advantage claims : Demonstrate improvements that are (i) statistically significant, (ii) substantial ( >5 pp), (iii) consistent across seeds, and (iv) observed on multiple datasets. 

Future work Several directions merit further investigation: 

• Hardware scaling : Extending hardware validation to higher qubit counts ( n = 10 −20) and more complex feature maps to test whether fidelity degrades gracefully or catastrophically. 

• Problem-specific feature maps : Designing quantum circuits informed by the data geometry or symmetry group [Glick et al., 2024], rather than using generic architectures. 

• Projected quantum kernels : Using partial trace operations to create richer kernel structures that may achieve better spectral properties [Huang et al., 2021]. 

• Error mitigation : Evaluating whether advanced error mitigation techniques (zero-noise extrapolation, probabilistic error cancellation) can improve hardware kernel quality be-yond readout correction. 

• Real-world industrial datasets : Testing quantum kernels on domain-specific classification problems (e.g. manufacturing quality control, predictive maintenance) where data geom-etry may differ from standard UCI benchmarks. 

Reproducibility The complete benchmark suite – comprising ∼18 700 lines of open-source Python code, 683 cached kernel matrices, 54 figures, and 132 unit tests – is publicly available to facilitate reproduction and extension of our results.

D.4 Computational Cost 

Table 33 reports the mean wall-clock time for computing a single kernel matrix (training split) across all main-benchmark and extended-study experiments, measured on a single CPU core with statevector simulation. Quantum kernel training (QKT) amplifies cost further, as each KTA optimisation step requires a full kernel recomputation; with 50–170 gradient 36 iterations per fold, the aggregate overhead reaches ∼2 000 ×.
