To what extent do individual eigenstates encode information of their underlying Hamiltonian, and how does this depend on their spectral position? For many-body quantum systems, this issue is widely understood in terms of the differing nature of the eigenstates near the spectral edges (low-entanglement, highly-structured eigenstates) and those far from the spectral edges (high-entanglement, near-random eigenstates). Utilizing the availability of machine learning tools, we introduce a new way to quantify the information contained in eigenstates: for a particular learning architecture, how precisely can the Hamiltonian be reconstructed from a single eigenstate? We refer to this property as learnability ; it serves as a new, alternative measure of the information content of eigenstates, made possible by machine learning. Using an encoder-decoder neural network and a physics-inspired loss function, we demonstrate how the distinction between two types of eigenstates is manifested as a difference in learnability . For spectral-edge eigenstates, the prediction accuracy is much better, and fewer eigenstates are required to learn the Hamiltonian, compared to mid-spectrum eigenstates. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Recent years have established machine learning (ML) as a powerful framework for quantum many-body physics, with applications ranging from variational rep-resentation of quantum states [1–5] or quantum state to-mography [6–9], to phase classification [10–18] and mate-rial discovery [19–25]. It has also been used effectively to improve numerically demanding classical tasks [26–28]. Beyond practical applications, a promise of an emerg-ing field is to provide new conceptualizations of physical phenomena. As an example from recent history, we re-call how the concept of entanglement entropies entered condensed matter physics at the turn of century [29– 32], opening up previously non-existent ways of thinking about many-body quantum physics. This included novel characterizations of phases, phase transitions, and dy-namics [33, 34]. In this work, we address a phenomenon of many-particle physics using a concept that only makes sense due to the accessibility of ML techniques for learn-ing from the available data. The phenomenon we address is a difference between many-body eigenstates near the ground state and those far from it. (We consider bounded Hilbert spaces, so we may select the extreme case of mid-spectrum eigenstates). It is well established that low-energy eigenstates of physical many-body Hamiltonians exhibit strong structure, described by locality [38] or constrained entanglement [33, 39]. On the other hand, mid-spectrum eigenstates are generally pseudo-random, consistent with the random matrix theory (RMT) predictions and the eigenstate thermalization hypothesis (ETH) built on top of it [40–44] with some system-dependent physical con-straints [45–52]. In Figure 1, we show how the prop-erties of eigenstates vary with energy by examining the entanglement entropy [(c),(d)] and the participation ratio [(e),(f)], illustrating a typical scenario — the nature of eigenstates is highly sensitive to their spectral position. In this work, we propose learnability as a quantitative probe of the information encoded in eigenstates, defined via the ability to reconstruct the underlying Hamilto-nian parameters using a fixed ML architecture. > III. LEARNABILITY

To what extent do Hamiltonian eigenstates encode model information, and how does this depend on spectral position? In particular, do eigenstates uniquely deter-mine the parameters of a given Hamiltonian family { ˆHθ }

[Eq. (1)], and how does this differ between low- and mid-spectrum states, when the latter often resemble random vectors? We now formalize the learnability as a quantita-tive measure of how accurately Hamiltonian parameters can be inferred from eigenstates. To this end, we address these questions using an au-toencoder architecture [54] that maps the eigenstates of a Hamiltonian { ˆHθ } directly to its underlying parameters 

˜θ. Our aim is not practical reconstruction, constrained by the curse of dimensionality [55], but rather to explore reconstruction’s fundamental limits. Specifically, we study a family of local Hamiltonians 

ˆHθ parametrized by θ ∈ RΘ, here Θ = 1 , and assess how well θ can be inferred from a subset of eigenstates following a chosen protocol, cf. Sec. IV A. Unlike HL and quantum state tomography [6–9], we do not assume access to parameter labels and rely solely on a physics-informed loss function, described below in Sec. III B.

The network is based on an autoencoder architecture composed of an encoder and a decoder . Conceptually, the training workflow is organized into three main stages de-picted in Fig. 2. > V. DISCUSSION A. Context and related work

ML has become a complementary approach to probe physical systems, for instance by identifying quan-tum correlations such as entanglement [68–73] and dis-cord [74]. More recently, increasing attention has turned to questions of interpretability [75–77] and physics-informed ML [65, 66]. In several cases, ML has been used as a diagnostic, matching or surpassing conventional in-dicators [78–80]. This suggests that ML can define data-driven indicators, providing a new class of probes on top of established theoretical approaches. Our central question was how much information about a Hamiltonian is encoded in its eigenstates. From a practical perspective, this is addressed by Hamiltonian learning (HL) [67, 81–86]. Its goal is to recover the governing operator, most often using measurements ac-quired either in a steady state [87–92] or during quan-tum dynamics [93–97]. HL has been successfully ap-plied in several areas, e.g., in analyzing transport mea-surements [92, 98–102], inferring tight-binding Hamilto-nians [103, 104], and characterizing spin models [88, 93] or quantum circuits [105–107]. A key challenge is the uniqueness of the Hamiltonian compatible with a given state. Most generic local Hamiltonians can be recon-structed from measurements of a single eigenstate [53]; see also Ref. [44] for related arguments. Subsequent work extended this to efficient reconstruction from local ob-servables [108] and quantified the number of independent constraints extractable from an eigenstate [109]. Simi-lar uniqueness guarantees have been obtained for Gibbs states and nonequilibrium steady states [110, 111], with the feasibility of reconstruction linked to locality and the decay of correlations [112]. Nonetheless, this perspective does not fully resolve how the available information de-pends on the spectral characteristics of the states. Several (already) conventional diagnostics characterize the structure of many-body eigenstates, such as entangle-ment entropy [33, 35, 39], correlation functions [113–116], 

compressibility [117, 118], and effective dimension mea-sures [119–121], depending on the community. In prin-ciple, eigenstates determine the Hamiltonian up to spec-tral ambiguities [43, 122, 123]. All of these measures, however, consistently indicate a sharp contrast between low-energy and mid-spectrum states [35, 43]. We have established a clear quantitative link between the spectral position of many-body eigenstates and the 

learnability of their parent Hamiltonian. Using an un-supervised encoder–decoder approach, we find that low-energy eigenstates preserve enough structure to reliably reconstruct local couplings, whereas this information is gradually and intrinsically lost as one moves toward the middle of the spectrum. This loss of learnability is largely independent of network capacity and instead reflects fun-damental properties of many-body eigenstates, in line with their crossover to thermal, pseudo-random behav-ior [43]. Our findings show that the feasibility of extract-ing the Hamiltonian structure is not uniform throughout the spectrum but dictated by the underlying physics of the states themselves. 7

> C. Conclusions

This work establishes learnability as a new information-theoretic framework for quantum many-body systems. It shifts the role of ML from being merely a computational tool to serving as a probe of the physical structure. In this way, our method offers a comple-mentary route to investigate how information is stored, preserved, and erased in complex quantum systems. This reframes learnability as an information-theoretic diagnostic rather than an inverse problem. Future work may address the notion of learnability 

across a variety of many-body problems. Of particular interest would be examining how it connects to the en-tanglement structure of eigenstates, the degree to which their information can be compressed [117], and the extent to which neural networks can generalize the underlying structure of eigenstates beyond the training domain.

> Appendix A: Neural network details

Here, we present a more thorough description of the network architecture used to obtain the main results of our study. To realize our methodology, depicted in Fig. 2, we employ the PyTorch framework [124] with single-precision floating-point arithmetic. Each optimization run is carried out by splitting the full dataset of size Nsam into a training set with Ntrain = 0 .7Nsam samples and a validation set with Ntrain = 0 .3Nsam samples. The code is publicly available upon request. The encoder (the only trainable component of the net-work), cf.
