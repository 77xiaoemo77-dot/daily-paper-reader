# Water-Filling is Universally Minimax Optimal ∗

Siddhartha Banerjee † Ramiro N. Deo-Campo Vuong † Robert Kleinberg †

Abstract 

Allocation of dynamically-arriving (i.e., online) divisible resources among a set of offline agents is a fundamental problem, with applications to online marketplaces, scheduling, portfolio selection, signal processing, and many other areas. The water-filling algorithm, which allo-cates an incoming resource to maximize the minimum load of compatible agents, is ubiquitous in many of these applications whenever the underlying objectives prefer more balanced solutions; however, the analysis and guarantees differ across settings. We provide a justification for the widespread use of water-filling by showing that it is a universally minimax optimal policy in a strong sense. Formally, our main result implies that water-filling is minimax optimal for a large class of objectives – including both Schur-concave maximization and Schur-convex minimization – under α-regret and competitive ratio measures. This optimality holds for every fixed tuple of agents and resource counts. Remarkably, 

water-filling achieves these guarantees as a myopic policy, remaining entirely agnostic to the objective function, agent count, and resource availability. Our techniques notably depart from the popular primal-dual analysis of online algorithms, and instead develop a novel way to apply the theory of majorization in online settings to achieve universality guarantees. 

# 1 Introduction 

Allocation of divisible resources (goods or chores) to agents is a fundamental algorithmic primitive. In many applications, the resources must be assigned dynamically as they arrive, under uncertainty about the future availability of resources and their compatibility with different agents, and in a way that is equitable. For example, a funding organization may allocate grants to eligible recipients over a sequence of time periods, without knowing the amount of funds that will be available for distribution in future periods nor which sets of recipients will be eligible. In any such setting, one may ask which allocation policies are minimax optimal, in the sense that their worst-case outcome is as equitable as possible. It would appear that this goal is underspecified; surely the answer must depend on the metric used to evaluate equitable allocations. The main message of this paper is that, despite the question’s ambiguity, it has an essentially unambiguous answer: the well-known and widely used water-filling algorithm, which greedily allocates arriving resources to maximize the minimum load among eligible recipients. To this point, the ubiquity of water-filling — also known as max-min fairness or resource balancing — has largely been explained through case-by-case analysis of different equity-promoting objectives. In each of the cases considered, the water-filling algorithm was shown to attain the best possible competitive ratio guarantee in the limit as the number of agents grows, often via primal-dual analysis. 1.1 Overview of Results and Techniques 

One can formulate worst-case analysis of dynamic resource allocation as a two-player zero-sum game between an algorithm designer and an adversary. The algorithm designer moves first by selecting a (deterministic or randomized) policy A for making allocation decisions given the available information. The adversary responds by constructing a sequence of requests E, where each request designates a quantity of divisible resources to be allocated and a set of eligible recipients. Running policy A on request sequence E leads to a load vector, A(E), specifying the total quantity of resources allocated to each agent. Formulating worst-case analysis in terms of α-regret is at least as general as formulating it in terms of competitive analysis: an algorithm is α-competitive if and only if the supremum of its α-regret is finite, and it is strictly α-competitive if and only if this supremum is non-positive. Our main result pertains to objective functions that obey the following minimal requirement to be considered equity-promoting: when one agent (Alice) receives weakly less resources than another (Bob), the objective value cannot be improved by taking resources from Alice and giving them to Bob. This property is called Schur-concavity (for maximization objectives) or Schur-convexity (for minimization objectives); we use the term Schur-monotonicity as a catch-all for both properties. We show that in the α-regret minimization game defined by any Schur-monotone objective and any non-negative comparison factor α, the water-filling policy is the optimal play for an algorithm designer against adaptive adversaries. When the adversary is restricted to be oblivious, 

water-filling remains optimal for the (narrower) class of symmetric concave maximization ob-jectives and symmetric convex minimization objectives. These results on the minimax optimality of water-filling persist even if the adversary is constrained to generate request sequences with a specified number of offline nodes, number of requests, and total amount of resources to be allocated. In other words, even though the water-filling algorithm has no foreknowledge of these parame-ters, nor of the objective function, it is minimax optimal even when compared against algorithms that have such foreknowledge. In summary, our results show that the worst-case analysis of dynamic equitable allocation for divisible resources produces surprisingly robust guidance for algorithm designers: water-filling 

is optimal against adaptive adversaries for essentially all equity-promoting objectives, and it is optimal against oblivious adversaries for a broad sub-class of such objectives. In particular, Schur-monotone objective functions include the most common objective functions for evaluating fractional allocations when agents are treated symmetrically. In fractional matching when all agents have equal capacity, c > 0, the objective function is f (x) = Pni=1 min( c, x(i)), which is Schur-concave. 2The Nash social welfare objective, ( Qni=1 x(i)) 1/n , is likewise Schur-concave. Common minimization objectives such as p-norms and the Gini index are Schur-convex. In Section 2.2 we give several examples of widely-studied objectives that fall in this class. To derive worst-case optimality results pertaining to such a broad range of objectives, we ab-stract away from objective functions altogether and directly compare load vectors using majoriza-tion , a preorder relation that compares the equitability of different allocations. Schur-convex (resp., Schur-concave) functions are precisely the order-preserving (resp., order-reversing) functions from the majorization order to the real numbers. Underpinning our results stated above is a more fun-damental result, Theorem 1, that expresses the optimality of the water-filling policy directly in terms of the majorization relation. Since majorization is a preorder and not a total order, a given set of vectors need not have a minimal or a maximal element. However, the worst-case analysis of 

water-filling is facilitated by the existence of such majorization-extremal elements in two impor-tant cases. First, in Fact 2, we show that the set of feasible load vectors for a given request sequence 

E always has a unique majorization-minimal element, which we denote by OPT (E). Second, in Theorem 3 we prove that for any specified hindsight-optimal vector ℓ, as E ranges over all the request sequences with OPT (E) = ℓ, the set of load vectors WF (E) produced by water-filling 

has a majorization-maximal element, and we give an explicit formula for this element. The minimax optimality of water-filling among deterministic algorithms is established via Theorem 1, which asserts that for any request sequence E, if an algorithm designer deviates from water-filling 

to some other deterministic allocation policy A, then the adversary can respond by modifying E

to a request sequence E′ that produces a less equitable load vector — EA(E′) majorizes WF (E)— but a more equitable hindsight-optimal solution, in that OPT (E′) is majorized by OPT (E). (If A is randomized and the adversary is allowed to be adaptive, we show in Section A that the same conclusion holds with probability 1 instead of with expectations.) Regardless of the choice of Schur-monotone objective function, the adversary’s response to the algorithm designer’s deviation makes the objective value worse at E[A(E′)] and better at OPT (E′), implying that worst-case 

α-regret increases when deviating from WF to any A. In the case of randomized A and obliv-ious adversaries, an application of Jensen’s inequality leads to the same conclusion provided the objective function is symmetric and concave (or convex). To prove Theorem 1 we take the perspective of an adversarial environment that designs the availability/compatibility of resources to hinder policies trying to produce majorization minimal allocations. The first part of the proof (Section 4) identifies a class of worst-case request sequences for water-filling , which are represented by nested edge sets on a bipartite graph. Our proof uses a series of combinatorial operations (Algorithm 1) to transform a given request sequence into one encoded by a nested sequence on which water-filling provides a majorizing allocation, and the hindsight optimal solution is minorizing. The second part of our proof (Section 5) considers a principal deviating from water-filling to play an arbitrary policy. We show that under such a deviation, the alternative policy produces a less equitable expected allocation than water-filling 

on request sequences encoded by a nested edge set. Combining these results, we find that water-filling yields a majorization minimal expected allocation when requests are generated by a worst-case adversary. 

1.2 Related Work 

Equitable Allocations and Majorization Majorization is a well-established tool for equitable allocation in economics Dutta and Ray (1989) and operations research Veinott Jr (1971); Megiddo (1974). More recently, it has been applied to offline integral allocation Harvey et al. (2003), ap-proximate online load balancing Bhargava et al. (2001); Goel et al. (2005); Kumar and Kleinberg 3(2006), and a variety of one-shot strategic decision-making settings Banerjee et al. (2024, 2025); Bai et al. (2026). Our work follows in this line, extending this theory to online fractional allocation. 

Online Matching and Generalizations The seminal work of Karp et al. (1990) on online bi-partite matching has led to a vast follow-up literature; see Mehta et al. (2013) for a survey. Much of this focuses on integral allocations, and uses online primal-dual methods Devanur et al. (2013); Buchbinder and Naor (2009). The most relevant work for us is Feige (2020), which analyzes water-filling for fractional matching and identifies the complete upper-triangular graph as the worst-case input among instances containing a perfect matching. Some of our arguments in Section 4 closely mirror those in Feige (2020), and in a sense, by using majorization, our work generalizes his result to a wide variety of settings beyond matching, and also suggests natural extensions of the minimax conjecture of Karp et al. (1990) (though these are still beyond our techniques). 

Fractional Allocation and Concave Utilities A more closely related line is that initiated by Devanur and Jain (2012) on online allocation with concave utilities. The original work provided asymptotically-optimal competitive ratios via solutions to certain differential equations. More recent works Hathcock et al. (2024); Patton (2026) provide asymptotic 1 − 1/e bounds for a wider range of objectives. All these results use dual fitting approaches, which can often obscure the underlying intuition. Indeed, Devanur and Jain (2012) explicitly comment that the exact bounds are “a bit of a mystery”. We hope our work sheds some light on how these specific bounds emerge. 

water-filling and Scheduling Our work also relates to the literature on universal guarantees 

in scheduling, where a single policy simultaneously does well across multiple objectives. This was pioneered by Kleinberg et al. (1999), who characterized prefix-based fairness measures via ma-jorization. More recent work achieves simultaneous competitiveness for all ℓp norms Chakrabarty and Swamy (2019a,b); Kesselheim et al. (2023), and stronger guarantees using predictions Cohen and Panigrahi (2023). The last of these noted their approach had parallels to work on online Nash welfare maximization Banerjee et al. (2022); our work suggests a rationale behind this observa-tion. These works all consider more complex constraint spaces than ours, and hence have weaker guarantees. 

Exact Minimax-Optimal Online Algorithms Finally, we briefly comment on the rarity of 

exact minimax solutions in online algorithms – results where an algorithm’s performance perfectly matches the theoretical value of the game on input sequences of bounded length. Notable examples include online prediction with 2 or 3 ‘experts’ Cover (1965); Gravin et al. (2016), unconstrained linear optimization McMahan and Abernethy (2013), ski-rental Karlin et al. (1994), and paging Sleator and Tarjan (1985); Achlioptas et al. (2000). We situate water-filling for online fractional allocation within this select family, noting its unique complexity and universality.

In this section, we present an explicit procedure (Algorithm 1) that transforms an arbitrary request sequence E into a nested one eE, such that the outcome of water-filling moves higher in the majorization preorder from E to eE, while that of the hindsight-optimal allocation moves lower.

Based on this, our transformation combines a pre-processing routine that transforms any given sequence E to engineer these two properties, followed by taking the induced nested sequence. We specify the full transformation in Algorithm 1, and in Figure 5, we illustrate our procedure on the example from Figure 1; the latter may be useful to refer to when reading the following. At a high level, our transformation proceeds as follows: first, inactive edges are removed, with no effect on the water-filling allocation (Proposition 1). Second, online nodes are permuted so 12 that they arrive in non-decreasing order of their height under water-filling , with ties broken so that the permutation reverses the arrival order of nodes whose water-filling heights are equal. Such a permutation of online nodes’ arrival order also turns out to preserve the water-filling 

allocation, although establishing this is more involved (see Proposition 2). The key observation is that the water-filling allocation is preserved only under online node permutations that ensure that each pair of online nodes that have a common (offline) neighbor remains in the same order. At this point, we can use Lemma 3 to get a new sequence eE such that WF (E) ⪯ WF e(E). To complete the analysis of Algorithm 1, we also need to show that OPT (E) ⪰ OPT ( eE). This is not immediate, as in step 1, we remove inactive edges, which could affect the feasibility of the optimal hindsight allocation. However, we show that any inactive edge ( i, t ) removed in step 1 is replaced with the equivalent edge ( i, σ (t)) in step 3 (Proposition 3). The proof of this fact makes essential use of the arrival-order-reversing tie-breaking rule in step 2. In Section 4.2, we specify our transformation in Algorithm 1, and formalize the above arguments (via Propositions 1, 3 and 5) and use these to prove Lemma 1. Due to space constraints, we defer proofs of the intermediate propositions to Section B.3. Given the centrality of Lemma 3 in our approach, we provide a complete proof for it in section 4.3.

# A Adaptive Adversaries 

In the main body of our work, we focused on an oblivious adversary that designs the entirety of the request sequence E ∈ E n,m,q without knowledge of the allocations produced by the algorithm designer’s policy A. Against such adversaries, Corollary 1 and Theorem 2 show there is an unavoid-able separation between the classes of objections for which water-filling is minimax optimal that depends on whether or not the algorithm designer is allowed randomness. This separation arises because a randomized policy can, with non-zero probability, guess an equitable allocation on any fixed request sequence. In this section, we turn our attention to a stronger adaptive adversary who can dynamically design the request sequence depending on the play of the algorithm designer’s policy. An adaptive adversary, when the algorithm designer deploys a deterministic policy such as water-filling , is no stronger than an oblivious one. However, adaptive adversaries can prevent randomized policies from guessing equitable allocations. As a result, there is no separation between the deterministic and randomized minimax guarantees of water-filling against adaptive adversaries. 

A.1 Adaptive Adversary Formalization 

We use a similar game setting to the one described in Section 2.1. However, instead of being a static request sequence, the adversary’s policies are maps from a history of past requests (online nodes) and the algorithm designer’s allocations on those nodes to the next online node in the sequence.
