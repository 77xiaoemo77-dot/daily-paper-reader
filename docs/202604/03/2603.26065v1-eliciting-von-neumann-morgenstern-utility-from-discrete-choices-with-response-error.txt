# Eliciting Von Neumann–Morgenstern utility from discrete choices with response error 

## Bo Chen 1 and Jia Liu 1* 

> 1

School of Mathematics and Statistics, Xi’an Jiaotong University, Xi’an, Shaanxi, China. *Corresponding author(s). E-mail(s): jialiu@xjtu.edu.cn; Contributing authors: chenbo1211@stu.xjtu.edu.cn; 

Abstract 

We develop a preference elicitation method for a Von Neumann-Morgenstern (VNM)-type decision-maker from pairwise comparison data in the presence of response errors. We apply the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) method to jointly elicit the non-parametric systematic VNM utility function and the scale parameter of the response error, assum-ing a Gumbel distribution. We incorporate structural preference information known in advance about the decision-maker’s risk attitude through linear constraints on the util-ity function, including monotonicity, concavity, and Lipschitz continuity. Under discretely distributed lotteries, the resulting MLE problem can be reformulated as a convex pro-gram. We derive finite-sample error bounds between the MLE and the true parameters, and establish quantitative convergence of the MLE-based VNM utility function to the true utility function in the sense of the Kolmogorov distance under some conditions on the lotteries. These conditions may have potential applications in the design of efficient lotteries for preference elicitation. We further show that the optimization problem max-imizing the expected MLE-based VNM utility is robust against the response error and estimation error in a probabilistic sense. Numerical experiments in a portfolio optimization application illustrate and support the theoretical results. 

Keywords: Von Neumann–Morgenstern expected utility, Pairwise comparison, Preference elicitation, Response error, Additive random utility model 

# 1 Introduction 

Discrete choices arise when a decision-maker (DM) selects from a finite set of alternatives and are typically observed through pairwise or k-wise comparisons or ratings. Among these, pairwise comparisons are the most common and play an important role in understanding and modeling individual preferences in behavioral economics and marketing. Such pairwise comparison data are widely used to elicit, for example, a reward function in Large Language Models [57], an ordinal utility function in recommendation systems [15], or a cardinal utility function in finan-cial decision-making problems [10], all of which are well-known quantitative representations of a DM’s preference. In practice, however, information about the DM’s preferences is often incomplete; thus, preference elicitation is a crucial step toward learning the decision-making process. 1

> arXiv:2603.26065v1 [math.OC] 27 Mar 2026

A major line of research on preference elicitation assumes that the DM provides consistent pairwise comparison responses (i.e., making the same choice among the alternatives in identical situations) and that there exists a real-valued utility function representing the DM’s true prefer-ences and hence observed choices. Under this assumption, consistent pairwise comparison data can be used to construct polyhedral ambiguity sets. These sets can then be exploited to generate new queries to shrink the ambiguity set, or to support preference robust optimization (PRO) for decision making based on the worst-case utility function within the ambiguity set. From the per-spective of questionnaire generation, Toubia et al. [61, 63] propose a class of polyhedral methods that leverage geometric properties to generate informative queries that more effectively elicit a multi-attribute linear utility function. Zhang et al. [76] propose a modified polyhedral method to elicit a DM’s nonlinear univariate utility function, which does not rely on explicit infor-mation about the functional shape or the inflection point of the utility function. Armbruster and Delage [2], as well as Hu and Mehrotra [28], are among the first to incorporate pairwise comparison data into PRO under a non-parametric Von Neumann-Morgenstern (VNM) utility framework. A major contribution of this line of work is to incorporate structural preference information about the DM’s VNM utility function known in advance, in addition to the con-sistent pairwise comparison data, in the decision-making problem. Such structural preference information may include for example, monotonicity consistent with first-order stochastic dom-inance [13] and concavity consistent with second-order stochastic dominance [52]. Numerous studies within the PRO framework incorporate further preference information (e.g., Lipschitz continuity and moment-type conditions) into the DM’s utility function and develop tractable reformulations of the resulting utility-maximization problems, together with theoretical analy-sis; see, e.g., [10, 23, 27, 30, 32, 33, 38, 67, 70, 76]. Meanwhile, studies on robust risk optimization adopt a closely related paradigm; see, e.g., [12, 22, 79]. However, this line of research pays limited attention to random utility functions and random response errors. In practice, the DM sometimes exhibits inconsistent choices in pairwise comparisons, even under repeated queries. Empirical evidence measured in a model-free manner shows that con-sistency in risky choices is about 75–85% on average [24]. There are two common sources of such inconsistent choices/behavior in the DM’s choices: state-dependent utility and response error. In some cases, the DM’s utility function is assumed to vary with the environment, referred to as the state or the contextual variable. Under this assumption, the modeler may assign a set of deterministic utility functions to each state, together with a probability measure over the random states. 1

In some other cases, it is believed that the DM holds a true discrete choice model or a deterministic utility function representing her/his underlying true preference. However, she/he might provide responses that are biased relative to the true preferences when responding to a query, known as response error [47]. The most widely used discrete choice model accounting for response error is the Bradley-Terry-Luce (BTL) model [41], also known as the binary logit model. When the DM is presented with random alternatives (i.e., risky lotteries), the corresponding choice model involves both the outcome uncertainty arising from the realization of lotteries and the response error from the DM, making the model more complicated than in settings with deterministic outcomes. For discrete choices over risky lotteries in the absence of response error, the well-known Von Neumann–Morgenstern (VNM) expected utility theory provides a flexible modeling framework for capturing the DM’s preferences over random gains and losses through a VNM utility function. 6 This raises the question of whether a VNM-type utility representation can be established for discrete choices over risky lotteries in the presence of response error. Eliashberg and Hauser [14] introduce response error in preference elicitation over risky lotteries by positing a VNM utility function u(x, r ), where the random risk parameter r is assumed to follow a parametric distribution f (r|λ), thereby capturing measurement error. In this paper, we continue the research on the preference elicitation of VNM utility functions from discrete choices among risky lotteries with additive response error. We develop a non-parametric VNM utility model with additive response error U (·) = E[u(·)] + ε, where the corresponding elicitation problem is formulated as a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) problem. We analyze the existence and uniqueness of the MLE solution. Furthermore, we show that the MLE-based VNM utility achieves a good estimate by establishing theoretical convergence results in the ℓ2 and ℓ∞ statistical errors relative to the DM’s true utility values at the breakpoints. • We incorporate structural preference information about the DM’s VNM utility function as linear constraints in the MLE formulation. Numerical results demonstrate its effectiveness compared with the purely data-driven approach that uses only binary choice data. 

• We analyze the existence and uniqueness of the MLE solution and establish convergence guarantees for our elicitation approach under suitable conditions on the queries. 9 We further interpret the statistical results and demonstrate that the resulting finite-sample error bounds provide principled guidance for designing pairwise comparison lotteries.

In Section 4, the MLE-based VNM utility function is applied to a portfolio optimization problem. Section 5 presents a set of numerical tests with a simulated DM to examine the performance of the proposed preference elicitation approach. Section 6 concludes.

Remark 1 The constraints in (2) encode some structural preference information about the DM’s true VNM utility function. (i) Under VNM expected utility theory, for any rational DM, there exists an increasing function u

such that, for any two lotteries X, Y , the DM prefers X to Y if and only if E[u(X)] ≥ E[u(Y )]. The monotonicity of u is consistent with the idea that most DMs prefer a lottery with a higher sure outcome to one with a lower sure outcome. (ii) The concavity of u corresponds to the risk-averse attitude of the DM, who has diminishing marginal utility in outcomes. Under the VNM utility theory, the utility functions of risk-averse DMs are typically increasing and concave. A risk-averse DM prefers a certain outcome to a risky one with the same expected value. In economics, introducing concave utility functions for rational DMs solves the St. Petersburg paradox and remains the central pillar of modern economic theory [75]. Concavity and monotonicity are also imposed in semi-parametric elicitation methods for additive random utility models, where the systematic utility for deterministic outcomes is modeled non-parametrically and the random error is modeled parametrically [43]. (iii) The Lipschitz continuity of u ensures bounded marginal utility, in the sense that the DM’s utility cannot change too rapidly near any specific outcome [28]. This condition is mild and is satisfied by most utility functions used in the literature, for example, all law-invariant coherent risk measures are globally Lipschitz continuous over random losses [34]. This condition is also related to the robustness of risk measures under data perturbations in data-driven problems [21, 71]. The Lipschitz modulus L can be interpreted and estimated as the maximum cost the DM is willing to pay for a marginal unit of outcome when possessing nothing. (iv) Given the concavity and Lipschitz continuity of u, its range over [0 , ¯b] is bounded by L¯b. Such bounds are often used for normalization, which does not change the preference ranking between any two alternatives. The normalization condition also prevents the non-uniqueness caused by linear transformations [32].

Second, this separation maintains a general modeling framework that accommodates broader data types and preference models, as the adjustment step ˜ u=u/σ may not be valid for other data types (e.g., ratings), alternative risk measures (e.g., variance), or more general utility functions (e.g., S-shaped utility functions). Finally, from an interpretive perspective, allowing a separate variable σfor the response error εcorresponds to a DM-specific level of inconsistency in making choices. We leave these related issues for future research.

83 Preference elicitation from pairwise comparison data 

In this section, we consider the problem of preference elicitation from pairwise comparison data. We assume that the DM’s choice behavior can be described by the VNM utility model with response error U (Y ) = E[u(Y )]+ ε, as in Assumption 1. Our objective is to infer the VNM utility function u(·) and the distribution of the response error (parameterized by σ) from the observed pairwise comparison data. We first formulate the preference elicitation task as a maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) problem posed over an infinite-dimensional function space. To address the computational chal-lenges posed by the non-parametric structure of u, we show in Proposition 1 that when the lotteries in the pairwise comparisons are discretely distributed with finite support, the maxi-mum of the likelihood can be attained by a piecewise linear utility function. Building on this result, we reformulate the original infinite-dimensional MLE problem as a finite-dimensional convex program, as stated in Theorem 1. We then analyze the existence and uniqueness of the optimal solution to this convex program in Propositions 2 and 3. Furthermore, in Proposi-tion 4, we characterize the full optimal solution set in the original function space by extending the piecewise linear optimizer. Finally, we establish quantitative error bounds and convergence rates for the estimated utility relative to the DM’s true utility. The analysis explicitly accounts for both the random response error and the approximation error induced by the piecewise linear representation.

## 3.5 Interpretation and implications of the statistical results 

This subsection interprets the statistical error bounds established above. We explain the roles of the key assumptions (the informativeness of paired lotteries and the balance between the number of breakpoints N and the number of queries K) and highlight their implications for practical questionnaire design. 

Roles of N and K (model complexity and sample size). In our setting, K is the number of observed pairwise comparisons, while N := |Y| is the number of distinct payoff support points induced by these comparisons, which also determines the number of breakpoints in the piecewise linear approximation ˆ uMLE . As discussed before Corollary 2, the error bound in Theorem 3 consists of an approximation term and an estimation term. Under the dense support condition μN = O(1 /N ) in Corollary 2(a), the total error is of order O(1 /N ) (approximation) plus O(pN/K ) (estimation). This expression makes it clear that K plays the role of a sample size, while N plays the role of an effective model complexity (the dimension N − 1 of adjusted utility values at breakpoints). Such an interplay between model complexity and sample size is standard in statistical learning and non-parametric estimation, which reflects the classical approximation–estimation tradeoff [31, 64].

Balancing the number of queries K and the number of breakpoints N . To approx-imate a nonlinear non-parametric utility function, the set of breakpoints Y must be refined adaptively, which means that new breakpoints are gradually introduced and the dimension of the adjusted utility values at breakpoints increases (e.g., from N to N + 1). However, such refinement immediately enlarges the parameter space. Even when the existing K queries fully span the previous reduced space RN −1, they generally fail to span the expanded space RN .Consequently, the empirical information matrix Σ D is typically rank-deficient in the enlarged dimension, and additional informative queries are required to regain identifiability. Moreover, merely ensuring λmin (Σ D) > 0 is not sufficient to guarantee statistical conver-gence of the MLE. From the bounds in Theorem 3 and Corollary 2, the total error scales as 

O(1 /N ) + O(pN/K ). For this total error bound to vanish asymptotically, it is necessary that 

N → ∞ and N/K → 0, that is, N = o(K). This rate highlights that each additional degree of freedom must be supported by a sufficiently faster growth in informative queries. This require-ment reflects the classical principle that model complexity must be controlled relative to the sample size. Interpreting K as the sample size and N as a measure of effective model complex-ity, the condition N = o(K) ensures that the approximation error and the estimation error decrease simultaneously. This scaling is consistent with the principle of sublinear growth of complexity measures in learning theory [64], and with the classical approximation–estimation tradeoff [31], which requires model complexity to increase slowly enough to preserve consistency in non-parametric estimation.

# 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose a preference elicitation approach for the non-parametric VNM utility model with response error. The elicitation is driven by the DM’s observed pairwise compar-isons and structural preference information known in advance. The resulting MLE problem is reformulated as a convex program. We establish finite-sample error bounds characterizing the convergence of the MLE estimates to the true utility function. We further apply the estimated utility function to a portfolio optimization problem, where the resulting decisions are shown to be robust to both response error and estimation error. To examine the performance of the proposed preference elicitation approach, we conduct some simulation experiments with a simulated DM, using synthetically generated pairwise lottery comparisons. The results indicate that the estimated utility function converges to the true utility function as the number of pairwise comparison queries increases. Compared to two benchmark methods, one of which fixes the scale parameter and the other of which ignores the structural preference information, our method achieves superior elicitation efficiency. We also study how the rank of the empirical information matrix associated with the pairwise comparison dataset affects the estimation errors, providing numerical support for the theoretical error bounds in guiding the design of lottery pairs. In this paper, the response error is assumed to be additive to the systematic VNM utility function and to follow a parametric Gumbel distribution independent of the exogenous uncer-tainty in the lotteries. If these modeling assumptions are mis-specified, the resulting MLE estimates may lose efficiency. Exploring alternative specifications of the response error distri-butions, or modeling its potential dependence on lotteries, constitutes a natural direction for future research. For example, Gaussian additive errors are widely used to model symmetric noise [49, 62], leading to the probit model. However, unlike the Gumbel case, the corresponding MLE problem under Gaussian distribution generally does not admit a convex reformulation, which may significantly increase computational complexity (see, e.g., [35, 39, 73]). It is also of interest to consider non-parametric response error distributions (e.g., general marginal distribu-tions studied in [48]) or distributional ambiguity within a distributionally robust optimization framework [50, 53]. However, such extensions would introduce substantial computational and statistical challenges. On the computational side, losing the parametric structure may pre-clude a finite-dimensional convex reformulation of the MLE problem. In practice, many human DMs may exhibit state-dependent preferences, whose utility representation varies across states. Our current framework assumes only a single invariant VNM utility function u in the systematic component. A natural extension is to consider 

U (Y ; s) = E[u(Y ; s)]+ ε, where s denotes an exogenous state or a contextual variable, and u(·; s)captures how the underlying preferences vary across states. Axiomatic work [37] provides the-oretical foundations for expected utility with state-dependent preferences, and future research could investigate the elicitation of state-dependent, non-parametric VNM utility functions [38] in the presence of response error. Finally, preference robust optimization (PRO) without response error has been extensively studied in the literature. Incorporating the proposed elicitation framework with response error into PRO constitutes a promising research direction. In particular, the MLE-based VNM utility function can serve as a nominal center, while the finite-sample error bounds may provide guidance for determining the radius of a ball-type ambiguity set. Alternatively, the feasible parameter set or an ϵ-optimal solution set of the MLE problem could serve directly as an ambiguity set in future extensions.
